Life of Siddhartha Gautama

▼
  • Born ~560 BCE as a Kshatriya prince in Kapilavastu. His father sheltered him from all suffering.
  • The Four Sights: An old man (aging), a sick man (illness), a corpse (death), a wandering ascetic (spiritual freedom). These confronted him with dukkha and inspired his spiritual quest.
  • The Great Renunciation: Siddhartha left his palace, wife, and child to seek liberation — mirroring Mahavira in method but differing in conclusions.
  • Extreme asceticism: Like Mahavira, he practiced harsh self-mortification — starving himself nearly to death. Unlike Mahavira, he rejected this as counterproductive.
  • The Bodhi Tree: Sitting under a fig/bodhi tree at Bodhi Gaya, he meditated deeply and faced temptation by the demon Mara (fear, desire, social duty). He overcame Mara and attained enlightenment → semi-nirvana (still alive).
  • The Middle Path: Between extreme indulgence (palace life) and extreme asceticism — neither too tight nor too loose, like a lute string.
  • First Sermon at Benares (Varanasi) to 5 former companions (bhikkhus) — taught the Four Noble Truths and Eightfold Path. This launched the first Sangha.
  • Death at age 80: Entered pari-nirvana (final nirvana at death) — no more rebirth.
IB Comparison Hook: Both Siddhartha (Buddhism) and Mahavira (Jainism) were Kshatriya princes who rejected caste privilege and practiced extreme asceticism — but Buddhism rejected the extreme (Middle Way), while Jainism embraced it (Five Mahavratas). Buddhism emerged ~6th century BCE as a reaction to Sanatana Dharma caste rigidity.

The Human Condition

▼

All humans are caught in samsara (cycle of rebirth and re-death), characterized by:

  • Maya — illusion/delusion: we mistake the impermanent for permanent, the self for a fixed entity
  • Tanha/Trishna — craving/desire: the root cause of our suffering and continued rebirth
  • Aversion/Hatred — pushing away what we dislike, rooted in the same ignorance as craving

These three (delusion, craving, aversion) feed the cycle of kamma/karma — actions that determine the quality of future rebirths.

Eschatology — What Happens After Death?

Theravada Goal: Arahant/Arhat

  • Enlightened individual who will not reincarnate after death
  • Attains nibbana/nirvana — cessation of craving, full liberation
  • The "selfish" path (critics say) — personal liberation only

Mahayana Goal: Bodhisattva

  • Enlightened being who chooses rebirth to help others attain liberation
  • Embodies karuna (compassion) above personal nirvana
  • Example: Avalokiteshvara / Kwan Yin (bodhisattva of compassion)
Two Types of Nirvana
  • Semi-nirvana (Nibbana while alive): Attained at enlightenment — cessation of craving/delusion while still living in a body. Siddhartha experienced this under the Bodhi Tree.
  • Pari-nirvana (Final nirvana at death): The complete cessation — no more rebirth. Attained at physical death after achieving enlightenment. Siddhartha's death at 80.

Three Marks of Existence & Five Aggregates

▼
Three Marks of Existence (Tilakkhaṇa)
Dukkha / Dukha — Suffering / unsatisfactoriness. The fundamental quality of conditioned existence. All things conditioned by craving lead to dissatisfaction.
Anicca / Anitya — Impermanence. All things are in constant flux. Nothing conditioned is permanent — attachment to impermanent things causes dukkha. The sand mandala demonstrates this vividly.
Anatta / Anatman — No-self / no-soul. There is no permanent, unchanging self or soul. What we call "self" is a combination of the Five Aggregates in flux. This directly contradicts the Hindu concept of Atman.
IB Contrast: Sanatana Dharma teaches Atman (eternal soul that reincarnates). Buddhism teaches Anatta — there is NO eternal soul. Ask: if there is no self, what is it that reincarnates? Buddhism's answer: the aggregates/consciousness stream, driven by karma — not a fixed soul.
Five Aggregates (Khandhas/Skandhas)

What we call the "self" is actually a combination of five constantly changing aggregates:

  1. Form / Matter (Rupa): The physical body — the material component
  2. Sensation (Vedana): Feelings — pleasant, unpleasant, or neutral responses to stimuli
  3. Perception (Sanna/Samjna): Recognition and identification of objects
  4. Mental Formation (Sankhara/Samskara): Volition, intentions, mental habits — the seat of karma
  5. Consciousness (Vinnana/Vijnana): Awareness; the basic knowing function

None of these aggregates, alone or together, constitutes a permanent self. They arise and pass away interdependently.

The Four Noble Truths

▼
Medical Metaphor — The "Great Physician": The Buddha diagnosed the human condition like a doctor: (1) Name the illness → (2) Find the cause → (3) Determine if cure exists → (4) Prescribe the cure.
  1. Dukkha — The Illness: Life is suffering. Three types of dukkha:
    · Physical pain and discomfort
    · The suffering of change and loss (impermanence)
    · Existential suffering from maya/illusion (even pleasant states are unsatisfactory because impermanent)
  2. Samudaya — The Cause: Dukkha arises from tanha (craving/desire). The 12-fold chain of dependent origination (Paticca-samuppada) explains how ignorance → craving → suffering → rebirth. Three roots of craving: greed, hatred, delusion.
  3. Nirodha — The Prognosis: Cessation of dukkha is possible. Nirvana/nibbana — the complete extinguishing of craving — is achievable. Two forms: semi-nirvana (while alive) and pari-nirvana (at death).
  4. Magga — The Cure: The Noble Eightfold Path / Middle Way. The practical road map to liberation.

The Noble Eightfold Path (Middle Way)

▼

Eight interconnected practices forming three categories — not a linear sequence but a holistic, simultaneous way of living:

Panna / Prajna — Wisdom
1. Right View (Samma Ditthi): Understanding the Four Noble Truths, the Three Marks of Existence, karma, and the nature of reality.
2. Right Intention (Samma Sankappa): Intention of renunciation (non-attachment), non-ill-will (loving kindness), and non-cruelty (compassion).
Sila — Ethical Conduct
3. Right Speech (Samma Vaca): No lying, divisive, harsh, or idle talk. Truthful and kind communication.
4. Right Action (Samma Kammanta): No killing, stealing, or sexual misconduct. The basis of the Five Precepts.
5. Right Livelihood (Samma Ajiva): No occupation that harms living beings — no weapons trade, no slave trade, no butchering, no intoxicant production.
Bhavana — Mental Discipline
6. Right Effort (Samma Vayama): Cultivating wholesome mental states; preventing and abandoning unwholesome states.
7. Right Mindfulness (Samma Sati): Mindful awareness of body, feelings, mind, and phenomena — the foundation of meditation.
8. Right Concentration (Samma Samadhi): Meditative concentration developing through the four jhanas (meditative absorptions) to deep stillness and insight.
Theravada Practice: Vipassana meditation (insight meditation) — systematic mindfulness.
Mahayana: Diverse — includes Zen (koan meditation), Pure Land (devotion to Amitabha), Vajrayana (mantra/tantra/mandala).

Three Jewels, Five Precepts & Lay Practice

▼
Three Jewels / Triple Refuge (Triratna)

The foundational commitment of all Buddhists — recited formally as an act of taking refuge:

"I take refuge in the Buddha" — the Historical Buddha as teacher and example of liberation
"I take refuge in the Dhamma" — the teachings, the Truth about reality
"I take refuge in the Sangha" — the worldwide community of practitioners (monastic and lay)
Five Precepts (Pañcasila) — Lay Buddhist Ethics
  1. Refrain from taking life (ahimsa principle — similar to Jainism but not absolute)
  2. Refrain from taking what is not given (no stealing)
  3. Refrain from sexual misconduct
  4. Refrain from false speech (no lying)
  5. Refrain from intoxicants that cloud the mind
Note: Buddhist ahimsa is NOT as absolute as Jain ahimsa. Theravada monks may eat meat if they did not see, hear, or suspect the animal was killed for them (three-fold purity rule). Jains are strict vegetarians. Some Mahayana traditions emphasize vegetarianism more strongly.
Sangha — The Community
  • Worldwide community — both monastic (monks/bhikkhus and nuns/bhikkhunis) and lay practitioners
  • Theravada: strong monastic tradition, monks as primary practitioners; lay support monks through dana (giving)
  • Mahayana: more emphasis on lay practice; bodhisattva path available to all

The Sangha Split: Theravada vs Mahayana

▼

Approximately 100 years after the Buddha's death, the Sangha split. The two main traditions diverged significantly:

☘ Theravada ("Way of the Elders")

  • ~38% of world Buddhists
  • SE Asia, Sri Lanka, Cambodia, Thailand, Burma
  • Ideal: Arahant — personal liberation, will not reincarnate
  • Scripture: Pali Canon only (Tripitaka)
  • Meditation: Vipassana (insight meditation)
  • Not strict vegetarians (three-fold purity rule for meat)
  • Simple, restrained art/images of the Buddha
  • Strong monastic tradition

🪷 Mahayana ("Great Vehicle")

  • ~62% of world Buddhists
  • China, Korea, Japan, Tibet, Nepal, Vietnam
  • Ideal: Bodhisattva — chooses rebirth to help others
  • Scripture: Pali Canon + Mahayana Sutras (Diamond, Heart, Lotus)
  • Diverse: Zen, Pure Land, Vajrayana, Chan
  • Elaborate art, thangkas, mandalas, rich iconography
  • Lay practice more accessible; bodhisattva path for all
  • Dalai Lama (Vajrayana/Tibetan), Kwan Yin/Avalokiteshvara
  • Uses upaya — skillful means (any method that helps beings toward liberation)
Mahayana Offshoots
  • Chan (China) / Zen (Japan): Meditation-focused; koans; direct experience over scripture
  • Pure Land: Devotion to Amitabha Buddha; rebirth in the "Western Paradise" / Pure Land as a stepping stone to nirvana
  • Vajrayana / Tibetan Buddhism: Uses tantras, mantras, mudras, mandalas; esoteric practices; Dalai Lama tradition

Sacred Texts

▼
Pali Canon / Tripitaka (All traditions)
Vinaya Pitaka: "Basket of Discipline" — rules for monastic life, codes of conduct for monks and nuns
Sutta Pitaka: "Basket of Discourses" — the teachings/sayings of the Buddha. Contains the Dhammapada (the most widely read Buddhist text — a collection of the Buddha's verses)
Abhidhamma Pitaka: "Basket of Higher Teaching" — systematic philosophical and psychological analysis
Mahayana Sutras (Mahayana only)
  • Diamond Sutra: On non-attachment and the emptiness of phenomena (sunyata)
  • Heart Sutra: On emptiness — "Form is emptiness, emptiness is form"
  • Lotus Sutra: On universal buddhahood and the bodhisattva path — foundational for many Mahayana schools
Vajrayana Tantras

Esoteric texts used in Tibetan Buddhism — include mantras, ritual instructions, visualization practices.

Three Dharma Seals (Mahayana)

Three marks that authenticate a teaching as Buddhist: (1) All conditioned phenomena are impermanent (anicca), (2) All conditioned phenomena are dukkha, (3) All phenomena are without self (anatta).

Rituals, Worship & Festivals

▼
Core Practices
  • Three Jewels Recitation: Taking refuge in Buddha, Dhamma, Sangha — the foundational Buddhist commitment
  • Puja: Devotional offerings (flowers, incense, light, food) before Buddha images — more common in Mahayana
  • Bhavana / Meditation: Central to all traditions — Vipassana (Theravada), Zen (Chan), visualization (Vajrayana)
  • Pilgrimage: To sacred sites — Bodhi Gaya (enlightenment), Benares/Varanasi (first sermon), Kapilavastu (birth), Kushinagar (pari-nirvana)
  • Dana (Giving): Lay people give food and support to monks; generates positive karma
Worship Objects (especially Mahayana)
  • Rupa: Buddha images/statues — focal points of devotion and meditation
  • Mudras: Sacred hand gestures of the Buddha — each has specific meaning (teaching, protection, meditation, earth-touching)
  • Thangkas: Tibetan painted scrolls depicting Buddhas, bodhisattvas, and cosmological diagrams
  • Mandalas: Sacred geometric diagrams representing the universe/cosmos — used in Vajrayana meditation; bell (awakening) and thunderbolt/vajra (enlightenment) are key ritual objects
  • Sand Mandalas: Mainly Mahayana/Vajrayana — intricately created from colored sand, then destroyed to demonstrate anicca (impermanence); sand thrown into a river, representing 4 directions; no two are the same
  • Stupas: Dome-shaped structures containing Buddhist relics — sites of pilgrimage and veneration
Key Festivals
Wesak / Vaisakhi (Theravada): Full moon of May — commemorates the birth, enlightenment, AND death of the Buddha (all believed to have occurred on the same day). The most important Buddhist festival.
Asala / Dhamma Day: Commemorates Siddhartha's conception, renunciation, and the first sermon at Benares.
Mahayana New Year: Celebrated in spring — varies by culture (Chinese New Year, Tibetan Losar, etc.)
Obon / Ullambana (Mahayana): Festival honoring ancestors and the departed — reflects the Mahayana emphasis on compassion for all beings across rebirths.

"Examine how the Four Noble Truths address the problem of suffering in Buddhism."

▼
Examine how the Four Noble Truths address the problem of suffering in Buddhism. [30]

Introduction

  • Define dukkha — the central problem of Buddhism (three types: physical, change, maya)
  • Introduce Buddha as "Great Physician" using medical metaphor
  • Thesis: The Four Noble Truths form a systematic diagnosis and treatment plan for the human condition

First Noble Truth — Naming the Illness (Dukkha)

  • All conditioned existence is unsatisfactory — even pleasant states end (anicca)
  • Three Marks of Existence: dukkha, anicca, anatta — why the self itself is a source of suffering
  • Five Aggregates as evidence: what we call "self" is a flux of impermanent processes

Second Noble Truth — The Cause (Tanha)

  • Tanha (craving/desire) as root cause — three roots: greed, hatred, delusion
  • 12-fold chain: ignorance → formations → consciousness → ... → craving → becoming → birth → aging/death
  • Kamma: how craving-driven actions perpetuate samsara

Third Noble Truth — The Prognosis (Nirodha)

  • Cessation IS possible — nirvana is achievable in this lifetime (semi-nirvana) and at death (pari-nirvana)
  • Theravada: Arahant ideal vs Mahayana: Bodhisattva ideal — different visions of liberation

Fourth Noble Truth — The Cure (Eightfold Path)

  • Noble Eightfold Path as the Middle Way — neither indulgence nor extreme asceticism
  • Three categories: Wisdom (Panna), Ethics (Sila), Mental Discipline (Bhavana) — holistic, not sequential
  • Example: Right Mindfulness/Concentration → Vipassana meditation (Theravada)

Conclusion / Evaluation

  • The Four Noble Truths are comprehensive: diagnosis + cause + possibility of cure + prescription
  • Evaluate: Do they fully address all suffering? Mahayana expands with bodhisattva compassion — the individual path is not sufficient
  • Contrast with Sanatana Dharma: moksha through union vs Buddhist nirvana through extinguishing — different solutions to related problems
💡 Always distinguish Theravada and Mahayana responses to the Truths — marks awarded for showing diversity within Buddhism.

"To what extent is the Theravada ideal of the Arahant superior to the Mahayana ideal of the Bodhisattva?"

▼
To what extent is the Theravada ideal of the Arahant superior to the Mahayana ideal of the Bodhisattva? [30]

Introduction

  • Context: Sangha split ~100 years after Buddha's death; theological and practical differences
  • Define Arahant (personal liberation; Theravada) and Bodhisattva (compassionate rebirth; Mahayana)
  • Thesis: The question of "superiority" depends on whether one prioritizes personal liberation or compassionate engagement — and both claim scriptural basis

Case For the Arahant (Theravada)

  • Closest to the Historical Buddha's own teaching (Pali Canon only)
  • Individual effort honors the Buddha's last words: "Work out your own salvation with diligence"
  • Cleaner metaphysics: consistent with anatta (no-self) — who is the Bodhisattva choosing rebirth if there's no self?
  • Vipassana: rigorous, monastic, evidence-based practice

Case For the Bodhisattva (Mahayana)

  • More consistent with the Buddha's own compassionate ministry (he stayed to teach rather than remain in private nirvana)
  • Upaya (skillful means): uses any method to help beings — more accessible to lay practitioners
  • Karuna (compassion) as the highest virtue — the Arahant is criticized as selfish ("Hinayana" / lesser vehicle)
  • Avalokiteshvara / Kwan Yin: iconic expression of universal compassion
  • Reaches more people (~62% of Buddhists) — arguably more effective in relieving suffering

Conclusion / Evaluation

  • Neither is objectively "superior" — both are valid paths with historical and scriptural grounding
  • The debate reflects a fundamental tension: individual liberation vs collective compassion
  • Evaluate using evidence: both aim at cessation of dukkha — they differ on means and scope
💡 Use the term "Hinayana" only to explain what Mahayana critics called Theravada — do not use it as your own label, as it is considered pejorative.

"Examine the role of the Sangha in Buddhism."

▼
Examine the role of the Sangha in the Buddhist path to liberation. [30]

Introduction

  • Define Sangha — the worldwide community of Buddhist practitioners, monastic and lay
  • Context: One of the Three Jewels (Buddha, Dhamma, Sangha) — central to Buddhist identity
  • Thesis: The Sangha is both the context for and the expression of Buddhist practice

The Sangha as Community

  • First Sangha: 5 bhikkhus at Benares after first sermon — foundational moment
  • Monastic Sangha (bhikkhus/bhikkhunis): dedicated practitioners; Vinaya Pitaka governs monastic life
  • Lay Sangha: broader community; practices Five Precepts; supports monks through dana

Theravada Role of the Sangha

  • Monks as primary practitioners; laypeople generate merit through supporting monks
  • Vipassana meditation communities; teaching lineages
  • Sangha as embodiment of the Dhamma in action

Mahayana Role of the Sangha

  • More inclusive — Bodhisattva path available to laypeople; Sangha not restricted to monastics
  • Bodhisattvas as Sangha members across rebirths — compassionate network
  • Diverse forms: Zen communities, Pure Land devotional groups, Tibetan monastery-universities (Nalanda tradition)

Conclusion / Evaluation

  • The Sangha provides structure, accountability, and teaching lineages essential for practice
  • Without the Sangha, access to the Dhamma would be lost — the Buddha specifically maintained it
  • Evaluate: Is the Sangha a means to liberation or an expression of it? Argue both

"Compare the Buddhist and Jain responses to the problem of karma."

▼
Compare and contrast the Buddhist and Jain understandings of karma and liberation. [30]

Introduction

  • Both Buddhism and Jainism emerged ~6th century BCE in the same region, as reactions to Vedic Sanatana Dharma
  • Both retain karma and samsara but radically redefine them
  • Thesis: Despite shared vocabulary, Buddhist and Jain karma differ fundamentally in metaphysics and in the path to liberation

Buddhist Karma

  • Kamma = volitional action — it is intention (cetana) that determines karmic weight
  • Not material — karma is a moral/energetic force shaping the stream of consciousness/aggregates
  • Consistent with anatta: no permanent soul carries karma — the stream of conditioned consciousness does
  • Path: Noble Eightfold Path ceases karma-generating craving → nirvana

Jain Karma

  • Karma is MATERIAL particles — literally physical matter that sticks to the Jiva (soul)
  • Even unintentional action produces karma — intention matters less; the action itself leaves residue
  • Ahimsa is absolute because ANY killing generates material karma regardless of intent
  • Path: Five Anuvratas + Triple Gems → shed accumulated karma + prevent new karma → Moksha (Jiva freed, ascends to Siddhashila)

Liberation Compared

  • Both traditions reject Vedic ritual/sacrifice as a valid path
  • Buddhism: nirvana = extinguishing of craving; anatta means no permanent self achieves it
  • Jainism: moksha = Jiva permanently freed from all karma, existing in perfect knowledge/bliss at top of universe
  • Key difference: Buddhism denies a permanent soul; Jainism affirms it

Conclusion / Evaluation

  • Both provide systematic accounts of why suffering exists (karma) and how to end it
  • Buddhism: liberation is from craving, not from material karma; Jainism: liberation is from accumulated matter
  • These differences produce radically different ethics: Jain absolute ahimsa vs Buddhist contextual ahimsa
💡 This is a high-value cross-tradition essay. Showing you understand the metaphysical differences (material vs intentional karma) will impress examiners.

Examine the significance of the Three Marks of Existence for Buddhist practice. [30]

Introduction

  • Define Three Marks (Tilakkhana): Dukkha, Anicca, Anatta
  • Thesis: The Three Marks provide the foundational worldview from which all Buddhist practice flows

Dukkha — Practice Implication

  • Recognition of suffering motivates the spiritual search — as the Four Noble Truths describe
  • Without accepting dukkha, there is no urgency for the path

Anicca — Practice Implication

  • Impermanence → non-attachment → key practice of letting go
  • Sand mandala: deliberately created and destroyed — a ritual embodiment of anicca teaching
  • Right Mindfulness: observing the arising and passing of all phenomena

Anatta — Practice Implication

  • No-self → undermines ego-based craving → most radical teaching
  • Five Aggregates analysis: meditating on each aggregate as impermanent and not-self
  • Contrast with Sanatana Dharma (Atman) and Jainism (Jiva) — both affirm a permanent soul
  • Mahayana: anatta extended to all phenomena (sunyata / emptiness — Heart Sutra)

Conclusion / Evaluation

  • The Three Marks function together: dukkha arises from clinging to what is anicca while believing in anatta as a fixed self
  • Seeing the Marks clearly (Right View) is itself the beginning of liberation
  • Evaluate: Are the Three Marks universal truth-claims or culturally conditioned diagnoses? Engage with this critically.

Practice Paper 1 — Source Analysis

Buddhism · Use all sources provided · Answer all questions

Source A — The First Sermon at Benares (adapted from the Pali Canon, Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta)

"Monks, these two extremes ought not to be practiced by one who has gone forth from the home life. There is an addiction to indulgence of sense-pleasures, which is low, coarse, the way of the pworldling, ignoble, and unprofitable; and there is an addiction to self-mortification, which is painful, ignoble, and unprofitable. Without veering toward either of these extremes, the Tathagata has awakened to the middle way, which gives rise to vision, which gives rise to knowledge, and which leads to calm, to direct knowledge, to enlightenment, to Nibbana... Now this, monks, is the noble truth of dukkha: birth is dukkha, aging is dukkha, sickness is dukkha, death is dukkha..."

Source B — The Sand Mandala (adapted from a Tibetan Buddhist teacher's explanation)

"The construction of the sand mandala takes many days. Monks work in precise, meditative concentration, placing colored sand grain by grain to form an intricate geometric diagram representing the cosmos and the mind of the awakened one. When it is finally completed — beautiful and perfect — it is immediately destroyed. The colored sands are swept together and poured into a river, that the blessings may flow out to all beings in the four directions. No two mandalas are the same. This is our teaching on anicca — all things that arise must pass away. The beauty of the mandala is not diminished by its destruction; it is completed by it."

Question 1 3 marks
According to Source A, what is the "middle way" and why does the Buddha teach it?
Model Answer: According to Source A, the Middle Way is the path between two extremes: (1) addiction to sense-pleasures ("low, coarse, the way of the worldling") and (2) addiction to self-mortification ("painful, ignoble, and unprofitable"). The Buddha (referred to as "the Tathagata") teaches it because it "gives rise to vision, which gives rise to knowledge, and which leads to calm, to direct knowledge, to enlightenment, to Nibbana." The Middle Way is thus taught as the effective path to liberation, in contrast to the two ineffective extremes Siddhartha himself had tried — palace luxury and extreme asceticism.
Question 2 5 marks
Using your own knowledge, explain the Four Noble Truths as a complete framework for addressing suffering. Reference Source A in your answer.
Model Answer: The Four Noble Truths form a systematic diagnosis and cure for human suffering — the Buddha is often called the "Great Physician." The First Truth (Dukkha) names the illness: suffering is the fundamental characteristic of conditioned existence. Source A illustrates this directly: "birth is dukkha, aging is dukkha, sickness is dukkha, death is dukkha." This is not pessimism but honest diagnosis. The Second Truth (Samudaya) identifies the cause: tanha (craving/desire), rooted in ignorance, greed, and hatred. The Third Truth (Nirodha) offers prognosis: cessation is possible. Nirvana — the extinguishing of craving — is achievable in this lifetime (semi-nirvana) and at death (pari-nirvana). The Fourth Truth (Magga) prescribes the cure: the Noble Eightfold Path, the Middle Way between indulgence and asceticism, as Source A describes. This framework makes Buddhism uniquely practical: suffering is real, its cause is knowable, its end is possible, and the path is described.
Question 3 5 marks
Using Source B and your own knowledge, explain how the sand mandala illustrates the Buddhist teaching of anicca (impermanence). How does this connect to the broader goal of liberation?
Model Answer: Source B presents the sand mandala as a deliberate ritual embodiment of anicca (impermanence). The mandala is painstakingly created with "colored sand grain by grain" over many days — requiring immense effort and concentration — then immediately destroyed. This destruction is not a failure but the point: "all things that arise must pass away." The source explicitly states: "The beauty of the mandala is not diminished by its destruction; it is completed by it." This teaching connects directly to the Three Marks of Existence (Tilakkhana). Anicca (impermanence) is the second Mark: all conditioned phenomena are impermanent. Attachment to what is impermanent causes dukkha (the first Mark). The mandala ritual trains practitioners to observe beauty, appreciate it fully, and then release it without clinging — this non-attachment is the practical antidote to tanha (craving). The sand being poured into a river to "flow out to all beings in the four directions" also reflects the Mahayana ideal of karuna (compassion) — liberation is not merely personal. The practice is primarily Mahayana/Vajrayana, illustrating how that tradition uses upaya (skillful means) — physical ritual — to convey philosophical insight about impermanence and the path to nirvana.
Question 4 10 marks
Using both sources and your own knowledge, compare and evaluate the Theravada and Mahayana approaches to achieving liberation in Buddhism.
Model Answer (Key Points):

Introduction: Both sources represent different strands of Buddhism. Source A reflects the earliest canonical teaching (Pali Canon, Theravada scripture). Source B represents Mahayana/Vajrayana practice (Tibetan Buddhism). Both seek nirvana/liberation from samsara but differ substantially in method, ideal, and community focus.

Theravada (Source A tradition): Focuses on the individual path — the Noble Eightfold Path leads the practitioner (ideally a monk) to become an Arahant: an enlightened being who, at death, will not be reborn (pari-nirvana). The emphasis is on personal effort, Vipassana meditation, and strict adherence to the Pali Canon. Source A's language addresses "monks" specifically, reflecting the monastic ideal. Liberation is personal and immediate.

Mahayana (Source B tradition): The sand mandala (Source B) illustrates Mahayana emphases: the ritual is communal, compassionate (sand poured for "all beings"), and uses elaborate physical means (upaya — skillful means) to convey philosophical insight. The Mahayana ideal is the Bodhisattva: an enlightened being who delays personal nirvana to help all sentient beings attain liberation. Karuna (compassion) is placed above personal liberation.

Evaluation: The Theravada approach is arguably closer to the Historical Buddha's original teaching and is philosophically consistent with anatta (no-self). The Mahayana critique — that the Arahant ideal is "selfish" — reflects genuine tension about whether individual liberation is sufficient. The Mahayana's bodhisattva ideal is more accessible to lay practitioners and has proven more culturally adaptive (~62% of Buddhists). Both, however, agree on the fundamental diagnosis (Four Noble Truths) and the necessity of the Eightfold Path. Neither tradition rejects the Pali Canon — the divergence is in what else is accepted (Mahayana Sutras) and in the scale of compassion expected.
