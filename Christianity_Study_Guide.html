Core Key Terms (15 Definitions)
Trinity
The three-fold nature of God as Father, Son (Jesus Christ), and Holy Spirit — a Christian form of monotheism
Incarnation
Jesus Christ as God in the flesh — God becoming human
Church
The whole community of Christians and individual congregations who worship Jesus Christ
Salvation
All believers will be saved and live in God's presence through Jesus Christ
Kingdom of God
Both an earthly and heavenly closeness to God through Jesus Christ — present and future
Atonement
Reconciliation between God and humanity through Jesus Christ — his death on the cross "atones" for sin
Resurrection
Jesus Christ's rise from the dead (literal and figurative) and that of believers — THE unifying factor of all Christian sects
Sin
Act(s) of rebellion against the will of God/Jesus Christ. Original Sin = the Fall of Adam and Eve
Redemption
Jesus Christ as redeemer through his death on the cross — deliverance from Sin
Repentance
Recognition of the need to be saved from sin by God's love through Jesus Christ
Reconciliation
Salvation as a result of atonement through Jesus; reconciliation of Original Sin (Adam and Eve disobeying God)
Grace
God's love for humanity and the means to salvation through faith in Jesus Christ — a gift, not earned
Logos
The Word of God incarnate (in the flesh) as Jesus Christ — usually shown in red or blue in the New Testament
Agape
The love of God (as Jesus Christ) for humanity: his teachings, miracles, willingness to sacrifice, acceptance for everyone
Sacrament
Protestant: outward sign of a blessing from God. Catholic/Orthodox: actual presence of God/Jesus Christ. Includes baptism, Eucharist, marriage
⚠ IB Key — Resurrection

The Resurrection of Jesus is the ONE thing that unites ALL Christian sects — Catholic, Orthodox, and Protestant all agree on this. It is the non-negotiable core of Christian belief. Know this for comparison questions.

⚠ IB Key — Sacrament Definition Varies

Protestants: a sacrament is a sign of God's presence. Catholics/Orthodox: a sacrament IS God's actual presence. This is a major theological divide with real practical consequences (especially around the Eucharist — transubstantiation vs. memorial).

Human Condition

1. What is the Human Condition?

God created humankind in God's own image. God gave humans free will, but through disobedience (Original Sin) humans became separated from God. This is expressed in the Bible through the story of Adam and Eve and the Fall (Genesis). God provided a means for humanity's salvation through Jesus Christ.

⚠ Compare with Judaism

Both Judaism and Christianity start with humans made in God's image (B'tzelem Elohim / Imago Dei). But Christianity adds Original Sin as a permanent stain requiring a divine savior. Judaism does not have this concept — humans are born morally neutral and choose. This is a key IB comparison point.

2. Where Are We Going?

  • Heaven — eternal union with God; accepted by ALL Christian sects
  • Purgatory — a place between Heaven and Hell; a Catholic belief; NOT accepted by all Christians (Protestants reject it, Orthodox have similar concept but different theology)
  • Hell — separation from God; accepted by all Christian sects (sometimes literal, sometimes more figurative depending on the believer)

3. How Do We Get There?

  • Believing in the presence of God as Father, Son, and Holy Spirit (the Trinity)
  • Believing in Jesus Christ as Saviour
  • Living by the teachings of Jesus Christ
  • Participating in the sacraments as appropriate to your denomination
⚠ IB Tip — Purgatory

Purgatory is a CATHOLIC-ONLY concept (a place for the not-yet-perfect to be purified before Heaven). Protestant Christianity outright rejects it. Eastern Orthodox have a similar but different concept. Never say "all Christians believe in purgatory" — examiner trap!

Historical Development
~50 CE
Christianity begins as a Jewish sect (origins sometimes dated pre-Jesus — see the Essenes). Saul of Tarsus (d. ~65 CE), a Jew who had a vision of Jesus, converts and changes his name to Paul. He spreads Christianity throughout the Mediterranean. Who is the founder? Complicated question.
100–200 CE
Ignatius of Antioch and Polycarp of Smyrna use Paul's teachings to spread Christianity. Marcion develops stereotype of vengeful OT God vs. loving NT God. Obsession with severe asceticism (no sex/food, less body = more spirit). Irenaeus: writes in favour of 4 Gospels; establishes creed; creates apostolic succession; establishes origins of Roman Church (later Catholics); declines Gnosticism.
~250 CE
Christianity is now independent from Judaism but practised very differently across the Mediterranean (status of Mary, women, prayer, gnosticism, Arians, Nestorians).
313 CE
Edict of Milan (Emperor Constantine) — mandates kind treatment of Christians. Note: St. Helena (Constantine's mother) is the actual driving force. Christianity is tolerated but not yet official.
325 CE
1st Council of Nicaea — nature of Christ, baptism, kneeling, separating Easter from Passover (not all decided here). Defines orthodoxy and heresy.
331–363 CE
Emperor Julian the Apostate — reverses Edict of Milan; reinstates paganism; tells Jews they can rebuild the Temple (they didn't).
380 CE
Emperor Theodosius I proclaims Christianity the official religion of the Roman Empire. Ends the ancient Olympics; removes depictions of nudity in art.
451 CE
Council of Chalcedon — defines the nature of Jesus: is he human OR divine? Answer: BOTH — fully human and fully divine. This becomes official Christian doctrine.
400–1600 CE
Christianity spreads through Europe — conversion from paganism is often forced (Clovis, Charlemagne). Pagan traditions absorbed into Christian holidays: trees, lights, Santa, Easter eggs, December birthday. "Final" canonization of the Holy Bible (OT and NT) ~1400–1600 CE.
1054 CE
The Great Schism — The Pope (Rome/West) and the Patriarch (Constantinople/East) argue over doctrine (prayers, beard length, communion ingredients) — but really it was about political power (Pope vs. Caesaropapist). Both parties mutually excommunicated each other. Result: Roman Catholic Church + Eastern Orthodox Church. Both claim to be the "true faith" and the "original."
1517 CE
Protestant Reformation — Martin Luther breaks the control of the RCC and permanently fragments Western Christianity. Within 100 years there are numerous Protestant sects.
Post-1500
Christianity becomes a world religion — spread to the Americas by the Spanish and Portuguese (Catholic) and British (Protestant). Christianity gains fewer converts in Africa (Islam dominant) and Asia. Becomes a world religion partly through higher literacy and colonial mission.
⚠ IB Key — What Do ALL Christians Share?

Despite all the splits, ALL Christian branches share: (1) Death and Resurrection of Jesus Christ; (2) The Trinity (Father, Son, Holy Spirit); (3) Jesus as saviour. What they do NOT all share: specific sacramental practice, the number/nature of sacraments, purgatory, the Pope's authority, etc. Note: baptism is NOT universal in exact form — it varies.

Catholicism — Rituals, Sacraments & Worship

All Catholics are Christians, but not all Christians are Catholics.

The Seven Sacraments (all very important, in order)

Sacrament 1 — Most Important
Infant Baptism
Cleanses the baby of Original Sin. The most important sacrament — you cannot receive the others without it.
Sacrament 2
First Communion
Age ~7/8. First time to confess sins to a priest and receive the Eucharist. Commemorates the Last Supper. Transubstantiation: bread and wine become the actual body and blood of Jesus. Eucharist is ONLY for baptized Catholics.
Sacrament 3
Confirmation
Age ~13/14. Strengthens the power of baptism and gives the gift of the Holy Spirit.
Sacrament 4
Penance / Reconciliation (Confession)
Obtaining pardon from God through a priest for sins the sinner is remorseful about. Allows return to full participation in the Eucharist.
Sacrament 5
Marriage
Male and female only. Must be in a church. One partner does not have to be Catholic. If a married man from another religion converts, he does not have to divorce to become a Catholic priest.
Sacrament 6
Holy Orders
Becoming a nun, sister, or priest. Requires celibacy of both genders. Women CANNOT be priests. If a married man decides to become a priest (from another tradition converting), he may not have to divorce.
Sacrament 7
Anointing of the Sick / Last Rites
Gives comfort to the dying or very sick. Includes last confession. Can be held more than once (in case you recover and don't die).

Catholic Worship

  • Public worship in a church is "required" — either at a Saturday afternoon service (after 4pm) or on a Sunday
  • Confession before taking communion; no eating an hour before. Weekly confession is NOT required.
  • A service can technically be held wherever there is a tabernacle (the box holding the Eucharist, with a lit candle beside it)
  • Catholic mass is consistent worldwide in Bible readings, message, and order of service
  • Prayer groups: meet in homes or church; focus on the Rosary (prayer beads dedicated to Mary) or Bible readings
  • Silent retreats encouraged several times a year (3–5 days)
  • Pilgrimages: to the Vatican, Israel, sites of miracles, graves of saints

The Process of Becoming a Saint

  • Step 1: Wait 5 years after death (usually)
  • Step 2: Become a "Servant of God"
  • Step 3: Show proof of a life of "heroic virtue"
  • Step 4: Verified miracles
  • Step 5: Canonisation by the Pope

A saint is a person who tried to be a "perfect" Jesus-like Christian. Saints are prayed through for help (e.g. finding lost things, help during struggles). Each saint has a feast day and speciality.

⚠ IB Key — Transubstantiation

Transubstantiation is the Catholic doctrine that the bread and wine of the Eucharist actually become the body and blood of Jesus Christ — not symbolically. This is one of the biggest divides between Catholic and Protestant Christianity. Protestants view it as a memorial meal. Know this distinction for IB essays on sacraments.

Catholic Liturgical Year & Holy Days
Advent
Preparing for the birth of Jesus. Lasts one month leading up to Christmas. Four Sundays of Advent.
Christmas
Celebrates the birth of Jesus on December 25. Note: pagan traditions (trees, lights, gift-giving) were absorbed into this celebration.
Epiphany / 3 Kings Day
January 6 — 12 days after Christmas. The 3 Magi visit baby Jesus. Not as important in Catholicism, but VERY important in Eastern Orthodoxy (Greek boys dive for a cross).
Lent
Begins on Ash Wednesday (February). 40-day spiritual preparation for Easter. Give up a vice and replace it with something spiritual. No meat on Fridays.
Good Friday
The day Jesus is crucified. Go to church; do the Stations of the Cross (re-live Jesus' last day); restricted eating. Somber observance.
Easter Sunday
Date varies (March/April). Celebrates Jesus' resurrection from the dead. THE most important Christian holiday — the Resurrection is the core of faith.
Pentecost
50 days after Easter. Celebrates Jesus giving the Holy Spirit to the Apostles. The "birthday" of the Church.
Saint / Feast Days
Happen daily — thousands of saints, each with a feast day. Celebrated differently worldwide based on culture and personal devotion.
⚠ IB Tip — Easter vs. Christmas

For Christians, Easter is theologically the most important holiday (the Resurrection is the core of faith) — not Christmas. Christmas is culturally dominant in Western countries, but Christmas without Easter is theologically meaningless in Christianity. IB examiners may test this distinction.

Sacred Texts

  • Old and New Testament — most sacred; Catholics have their own version
  • Written in Greek, Latin, and/or local languages
  • Interpretation of scripture is from the Church, not personal thoughts — the Church interprets for you (that's the Protestant approach: personal interpretation)
  • Reading of scripture was largely forbidden 12th–16th centuries; sometimes punished by death
  • Writings of Popes and/or saints — very important secondary authority
  • Catechism of the Catholic Church — manual of religious instruction (like the Sikh Rehat Maryada)
  • The Apocrypha — holy texts not always canonized but considered important Christian works; often ignored by Catholics but exist

Core Catholic Doctrines/Beliefs

  • The Trinity; Incarnation; Resurrection; Ascension into Heaven
  • Apostles' Creed and Nicene Creed
  • Original Sin; Satan; Eternal Life
  • Virgin Mary — Immaculate Conception (Mary born without Original Sin) and Virgin Birth of Jesus
  • A lot of "mysteries" — aspects of faith that exceed rational understanding
  • Cosmology: Genesis, Adam and Eve
  • Eschatology: 2nd Coming of Christ, Apocalypse, Final Judgement, Purgatory, Heaven, Hell

Ethical/Legal Issues (Catholic)

  • Absolutely no: euthanasia, embryo research, stem cell testing, IVF, abortion, birth control (natural family planning is OK)
  • No homosexual acts or gay marriage ("hate the sin, love the sinner")
  • No sex / living together before marriage
  • Divorce highly discouraged — must receive an annulment (the Catholic "Get") from the Vatican to remarry in the Church
  • Any marriage not performed by a priest in a church is not considered a valid marriage
⚠ IB Tip — Annulment vs. Divorce

Catholics do not "get divorced" in the Church — they receive an annulment, which declares the marriage was never valid. Compare to the Jewish Get. Both traditions treat marriage as deeply sacred and make dissolution very difficult — good IB comparison point.

Eastern Orthodox Christianity

Eastern Orthodox includes Greek, Russian, Ukrainian, and other regional varieties. They vary by language but can attend each other's churches. Regional Patriarchs (not a Pope) lead each national church.

Orthodox Sacraments (7 — equally important as Catholic)

  • 1. Baptism — infant; cleanses Original Sin
  • 2. Chrismation (★ KEY DIFFERENCE) — like Catholic Confirmation, but done immediately after or at the same time as Baptism (not age 13–14 as in Catholicism). Babies are chrismated!
  • 3. Communion — starts VERY young (must confess before communion). Non-baptized must leave the church before communion.
  • 4. Holy Orders — priests CAN marry (unlike Catholic); monks CANNOT — they are celibate. Patriarchs are celibate monks.
  • 5. Penance/Confession — more optional than in Catholicism; not a sin if you don't do it.
  • 6. Marriage/Divorce — no annulment needed (unlike Catholicism)
  • 7. Anointing of the Sick

Orthodox Worship

  • HEAVY use of icons (imagery of saints, Mary, Jesus) — icons are venerated but not worshipped
  • Similar to a Catholic service (communion) but no musical instruments — only singing and chanting
  • Often stand through the entire service (no sitting, no seats) — can be 2 hours
  • Non-baptized are supposed to leave before communion/Eucharist
  • Pilgrimages to monasteries for retreats

Orthodox Holy Days

  • Complicated — different calendars (Julian vs. Gregorian). Christmas sometimes celebrated on January 7
  • Epiphany (Jan 6) is VERY important — Greek boys dive for a cross in the water. Far more important than in Catholicism.
  • Easter is usually 2 weeks after the Catholic/Protestant date
  • Lent — follow a vegan diet for the WHOLE period (stricter than Catholic)
  • Saint days also very important; most holidays are similar to Catholic but with different names/dates

Orthodox Doctrines & Beliefs

  • Similar to Catholicism but with a lot less veneration of Mary
  • Prayer beads — but not the Rosary
  • Cosmology and eschatology similar to Catholic
  • No purgatory
  • Orthodox have not changed any aspect of their liturgy/belief since the 300s CE. They claim to practise true Christianity.
  • Their Old Testament has the most books (includes Apocrypha/Deuterocanonical books)

Orthodox Authority

  • No Pope — regional Patriarchs lead each national church
  • Not as geographically centralized — no Vatican equivalent
  • Priests can marry unless they are celibate monks or patriarchs
  • Women cannot be priests

Orthodox Ethics

  • No abortion (less strict than Catholic); birth control sometimes allowed with priestly approval
  • No homosexual acts or gay marriage
  • Conversion: speak with a priest, take classes, attend services
⚠ IB Key Differences: Catholic vs. Orthodox

Main distinctions: (1) Orthodox have NO Pope — regional Patriarchs; (2) Orthodox priests CAN marry; (3) Chrismation happens immediately after Baptism, not at age 13; (4) No purgatory; (5) Less Mary veneration; (6) Epiphany is MORE important than Christmas in many Orthodox communities; (7) Lent = fully vegan. These are the kinds of specific differences IB rewards in essays.

Discuss the role of the sacraments in Christian religious life, with reference to at least two Christian traditions. Paper 2
Expand ▼

Key Argument

The sacraments are the primary means through which Christians encounter God and participate in salvation — but their number, nature, and significance vary dramatically between Catholic, Orthodox, and Protestant traditions.

Introduction Points

  • Define sacrament: Protestant = outward sign of a blessing; Catholic/Orthodox = actual presence of God
  • This definitional difference is the root of many theological disputes
  • All Christians agree on Baptism and some form of Eucharist — differences lie in how

Catholic Angle

  • 7 sacraments — all very important; Baptism is most important (required for all others)
  • Transubstantiation: bread and wine literally become body and blood of Jesus
  • Sacraments as the actual presence of God — not symbols
  • Penance/Confession: sins forgiven through a priest (another mediated relationship with God)
  • Every major life stage has a sacrament: birth (Baptism) → first moral responsibility (First Communion/Penance) → adulthood (Confirmation) → vocation (Marriage/Holy Orders) → death (Anointing)

Eastern Orthodox Angle

  • Also 7 sacraments; real presence in Eucharist
  • KEY: Chrismation immediately after Baptism — infants receive the Holy Spirit from the start
  • Communion starts very young; must confess before; non-baptized leave before this part
  • Confession more optional; Marriage divorce permitted without annulment

Protestant Angle (contrast)

  • Usually 2 sacraments: Baptism and Communion
  • Eucharist as memorial, not transubstantiation
  • Martin Luther: sacraments must be commanded by Jesus AND have an outward element

Conclusion

Sacraments are the backbone of Catholic and Orthodox Christian life — they structure every phase of existence and make the divine physically present. For Protestants, they are meaningful acts of remembrance and declaration. The Reformation pivot on this question (especially the Eucharist) was the defining break of 1517. Understanding sacraments is essential to understanding Christian denominational diversity.

To what extent was the Great Schism (1054 CE) a theological rather than a political division? Paper 2
Expand ▼

Key Argument

While the Schism used theological language (filioque, Easter dating, communion ingredients), its root cause was a struggle for supreme authority between the Pope of Rome and the Patriarch of Constantinople — a fundamentally political dispute.

Theological Claims (surface level)

  • Filioque controversy: does the Holy Spirit proceed from the Father alone, or from Father AND Son?
  • Use of unleavened vs. leavened bread in communion
  • Date of Easter, kneeling on Sundays, beard-length of clergy
  • Both sides claim these are essential theological differences

Political Reality (deeper cause)

  • Pope vs. Caesaropapist: Who has ultimate authority — the Pope in Rome or the Emperor/Patriarch in Constantinople?
  • After fall of Western Rome (476 CE), the Pope filled the political vacuum in the West; the Eastern Patriarch operated under the Emperor's authority
  • Both sides mutually excommunicated each other — a political act as much as a spiritual one
  • Both immediately claimed to be the "true faith" and the "original" — classic power-legitimacy language

Both/And Conclusion

The Schism was both. The theological disputes were real but would not alone have caused the split — power and geography made unity increasingly impossible. The political dynamic (Rome vs. Constantinople) gave the theological disputes their urgency. In IB terms: the Schism illustrates how religious institutions are always entangled with political power — a key theme in the study of world religions.

"The concept of Original Sin is the most important doctrine in Christianity." How far do you agree? Paper 2
Expand ▼

Key Argument

Original Sin is the problem; the Resurrection and salvation through Jesus Christ is the solution. Without Original Sin, the entire edifice of Christian soteriology (salvation theology) collapses — making it a strong candidate for "most important doctrine."

Evidence FOR (Original Sin is foundational)

  • It explains WHY humanity needs a saviour — without the Fall, the Incarnation has no purpose
  • It explains the human condition: God created us good, but free will led to sin and separation from God
  • The entire sacramental system responds to it: Baptism (cleanse Original Sin) → Penance → Eucharist
  • It unites all Christian sects: all branches agree humanity is fallen and needs redemption
  • Irenaean theology vs. Augustinian theology: debate about Original Sin shaped Western Christianity

Evidence AGAINST (Resurrection may be more important)

  • The Resurrection is THE unifying factor of ALL Christian sects — even more universal than Original Sin
  • Jesus could theoretically have been sent to redeem humanity even without a literal Adam/Eve story
  • Paul writes: "If Christ is not raised, your faith is futile" (1 Cor 15:17) — the Resurrection is the explicit claim
  • Some liberal Protestant and Catholic theologians interpret Original Sin metaphorically — but still believe in the Resurrection literally

Conclusion

Original Sin and the Resurrection are inseparable — they form a problem/solution pair. Original Sin is arguably the most important anthropological doctrine (about humanity), while the Resurrection is the most important Christological doctrine (about Jesus). The statement is largely accurate but must be qualified: Original Sin matters because of what it necessitates — the salvific act of God in Jesus Christ.

Compare the understanding of the human condition and salvation in Christianity and one other religion you have studied. Paper 2

Key Argument

Christianity and Judaism share the same starting point (humans made in God's image) but diverge radically on Original Sin and salvation — revealing fundamentally different understandings of what is wrong with humanity and what fixes it.

Christianity — Human Condition & Salvation

  • Problem: Original Sin — Adam and Eve's disobedience permanently damaged human nature; all humans are born in sin, separated from God
  • Condition: Humans have free will but are fundamentally oriented toward sin (concupiscence); cannot save themselves
  • Solution: God's grace through Jesus Christ — the Incarnation, death, and Resurrection of Jesus atones for all sin; salvation available through faith and sacraments
  • Destination: Heaven (union with God) or Hell (separation from God)

Judaism — Human Condition & Salvation

  • Problem: No Original Sin — humans are born morally neutral. The constant struggle between yetzer ha-ra (evil inclination) and yetzer ha-tov (good inclination)
  • Condition: Humans are fully capable of choosing good — free will is not fundamentally damaged
  • Solution: Teshuvah (repentance) + observing 613 mitzvot + Tikkun Olam — no divine saviour needed
  • Destination: Olam ha-ba (vague world to come); focus is on THIS life

Key Similarities

  • Both believe humans are made in God's image (Imago Dei / B'tzelem Elohim)
  • Both have a strong ethical framework
  • Both believe in some form of divine judgement

Key Differences

  • Christianity: humans are fundamentally broken and need a divine saviour. Judaism: humans are capable of choosing good without supernatural rescue.
  • Christianity: afterlife (Heaven/Hell) is very defined and central. Judaism: focus is this life; Olam ha-ba is vague.
  • Christianity: grace is unearned gift. Judaism: covenant obligations must be fulfilled — it is more reciprocal.

Conclusion

Both traditions begin with the same material — humans made in God's image — but construct radically different theologies of the human problem and its solution. Christianity requires divine intervention (Incarnation, sacrifice); Judaism requires human effort within a covenantal relationship. These differences reflect different understandings of God, humanity, and the nature of sin itself.

Examine how Christianity has changed throughout its history, with reference to specific turning points. Paper 2
Expand ▼

Key Argument

Christianity has transformed itself multiple times through institutional power (Nicaea, Constantine), political schism (1054), and theological revolution (1517) — and yet its core (Resurrection, Trinity) has remained constant. Change has been institutional and liturgical more than doctrinal.

Key Turning Points

  • ~50–200 CE: From Jewish sect to independent religion. Irenaeus creates the canonical texts and institutional Church. Gnosticism defeated. The Christianity we recognize emerges.
  • 313–380 CE: From persecuted minority to official Roman religion. Constantine (Edict of Milan) + Theodosius I transform Christianity from underground to empire. Pagan traditions absorbed (Christmas, Easter symbols). Power fundamentally changes the Church.
  • 325 CE — Council of Nicaea: Christianity defines what it officially believes (the Nicene Creed). Orthodoxy vs. heresy established. The Church becomes a doctrinal institution.
  • 1054 CE — Great Schism: East-West split. Two versions of "true Christianity" emerge. Political power drives theological difference. Orthodoxy claims to be unchanged since the 300s.
  • 1517 CE — Protestant Reformation: Martin Luther breaks the RCC's monopoly on Western Christianity. Personal interpretation of Scripture; rejection of the Pope; fewer sacraments; new theology of grace (Sola Gratia, Sola Fide). Christianity permanently fragments.
  • Post-1500: Global spread through colonialism changes Christianity yet again — African, Latin American, Asian versions of Christianity develop their own expressions.

What Has NOT Changed

  • The Resurrection of Jesus — the one constant across all traditions
  • The Trinity — Father, Son, Holy Spirit
  • The authority of the Bible (however differently interpreted)

Conclusion

Christianity has changed enormously in institution, practice, ethics, and theology — yet its doctrinal core has proven remarkably durable. The history of Christianity is the history of competing claims about what that core actually requires. Change and continuity coexist — a dynamic tension that makes Christianity the world's most internally diverse religion.

1b. Using Source B and your own knowledge, analyse how the doctrine of the Incarnation shapes Catholic sacramental practice. [5 marks]

5 marks — analysis required
Model Answer:
  • Source B's core argument: If God took on flesh (Incarnation), then physical matter can carry divine grace. This is the theological basis for ALL 7 sacraments — physical actions (water, bread, oil, hands) become vehicles of God's presence.
  • Transubstantiation: Source B argues it is the "logical extension" of the Incarnation. If Jesus was truly God in flesh, then the bread and wine can truly become his body and blood — transubstantiation is not superstition but consistent theology.
  • Own knowledge — Baptism: Water is a physical element that, in Catholic understanding, actually cleanses Original Sin — not symbolically. The physical IS the spiritual because of the Incarnation.
  • Own knowledge — Anointing of the Sick: Oil physically applied to a dying person carries divine comfort and healing — again, matter + ritual = divine presence.
  • Protestant contrast: Protestants who reject transubstantiation (Eucharist as memorial) do so partly because they see matter and spirit as more separate — a theological difference that traces to different understandings of the Incarnation.

1c. Using Sources A and B and your own knowledge, evaluate the view that Christianity is fundamentally a religion of love rather than law. [5 marks]

5 marks — evaluation required
Model Answer:
  • Evidence FOR (love): Source A — God's motivation is love (Agape); salvation is a free gift, not earned by obedience. Source B — grace (unearned favour) is the means of salvation. Jesus' teachings: "Love God... love your neighbour" (Matthew 22:37–39) — the Two Great Commandments replace detailed legal codes.
  • Evidence FOR (love over law): Paul's letters: "For it is by grace you have been saved, through faith — not by works" (Ephesians 2:8). Christianity explicitly broke from Jewish law observance (613 mitzvot) — no Kashrut, no circumcision required.
  • Evidence AGAINST (law still matters): The 10 Commandments remain binding. Catholic moral theology is highly detailed (no abortion, no birth control, marriage law, etc.). The Church itself has extensive canon law. Sacraments must be performed correctly — ritual law matters.
  • Nuanced evaluation: Christianity emphasises love as the MOTIVATION and GOAL, but law as the STRUCTURE of practice. The Catholic Church has one of the most complex legal systems in the world (Canon Law). The Reformation brought more emphasis on faith/love over works/law — but even Protestant denominations have behavioural expectations.
  • Conclusion: Christianity is fundamentally motivated by love (Agape — God's gift, the response expected from believers), but it is not law-free. The relationship between love and law in Christianity is tension-filled and varies by denomination.

1d. Using Sources A and B and your own knowledge, discuss the significance of Jesus Christ in the Christian understanding of the human condition and salvation. [10 marks]

10 marks — extended response
Model Answer — Key Points to Include:
  • The problem Jesus addresses — Original Sin: God created humans in God's image with free will. Adam and Eve's disobedience (the Fall) introduced sin and separated humanity from God. This is the "human condition" — beings made for union with God but now separated. Jesus is God's response to this crisis.
  • Incarnation (Source B): "God gave his one and only Son" (Source A) — God takes on human flesh. This is not a workaround; it is the ultimate expression of divine commitment to humanity. Source B argues this makes matter capable of carrying grace — not just a theological idea but practically the basis of all sacraments.
  • Atonement — the Cross: Jesus' death is the atoning act. He takes on the punishment for human sin; reconciliation between God and humanity is achieved. Redemption = bought back from the consequences of sin. This is universal: "whoever believes" (Source A) — no ethnic, cultural, or ritual prerequisite.
  • Resurrection — the proof: The Resurrection is the evidence that death has been overcome; the promise of eternal life is credible. Paul: "If Christ is not raised, your faith is futile." The Resurrection is what unifies ALL Christian sects — Catholic, Orthodox, Protestant.
  • The sacramental response (Source B): Because Jesus was God in flesh, the physical world can mediate the divine. Baptism cleanses Original Sin; the Eucharist makes Jesus present again; Confirmation gives the Holy Spirit. The Christian response to Jesus is not abstract belief but embodied, sacramental participation.
  • Salvation — where we're going: Eternal life with God (Heaven). The human condition (separation from God by sin) is reversed by Jesus: union with God is restored. Hell remains a possibility for those who reject this offer — separation from God by choice.
  • Denominational variation: Catholic: faith + sacraments + works of love. Protestant: faith alone (Sola Fide). Orthodox: theosis — gradual transformation into likeness of God. All agree Jesus is the means; they differ on the mechanism.
  • Conclusion: Jesus Christ is not one element of Christianity — he IS Christianity. The human condition (sin, separation, mortality) is answered at every point by who Jesus is (God incarnate), what he did (atoned for sin through death), and what happened to him (rose from the dead). Remove Jesus and the entire framework collapses. Source A's "God so loved the world" is not a preamble — it is the entire theological programme in a sentence.
