⚠ IB Key — Jihad

The Greater Jihad (al-jihad al-akbar) is the inner spiritual struggle against one's own ego, desires, and sins. The Lesser Jihad (al-jihad al-asghar) is external — and only justified for self-defense or helping the oppressed. Terrorism is NOT legitimate Lesser Jihad under Islamic law. IB examiners specifically test this distinction.

⚠ IB Key — Shirk

Shirk (associating partners with Allah) is the gravest sin in Islam — the one sin that, if unrepented, is not forgiven. This is why Islam rejects the Christian Trinity so firmly: attributing a Son and Holy Spirit to God is, in Islamic theology, a form of Shirk. This is critical for IB comparison questions on monotheism.

Human Condition

1. What is the Human Condition?

Humans must strive to submit themselves to the will of God. Without divine guidance, they are likely to stray from the path of prescribed and prohibited actions that God has set out in order to prevent moral error. Deen and dunya must be kept in balance.

⚠ IB Key — No Original Sin

Islam has NO concept of Original Sin. All humans are born in a state of fitra — natural purity and innate faith in God. Sin is the result of individual disobedient acts, NOT an inherited condition. This contrasts sharply with Christianity (and to a lesser extent Judaism). There is no need for a divine saviour to fix a pre-existing human flaw — just submission and return to the straight path.

2. Where Are We Going?

  • Jannah (Paradise) — for the faithful; eternal obedience to God's will; a literal physical place
  • Jahannam (Hell) — by disobedience to God's will; reserved for those whom God has decided to punish for grave sins and not repenting. BUT: Allah can/will send some souls to Jahannam for a while then bring them to Janna — it is not eternal for all (unlike Christian Hell)
  • Barzakh — the period between death and Yawm al-din (Resurrection Day) where souls await with Allah

3. How Do We Get There?

  • Acknowledging Tawhid (the oneness of God) and submitting to His will
  • Believing in God, the scriptures, angels, jinns, and Akhira (life after death)
  • Living according to the Quran and the divinely inspired Hadith of the Prophet Muhammad
  • Practising the Five Pillars of Islam — obligations prescribed in the Quran for individual Muslims
Muhammad's Life & Early History

Religiously, Islam begins with Adam, Eve, then Abraham, Hagar, and their son Ishmael (half-brother of the Jewish Isaac). Historically, it begins with the Prophet Muhammad.

570 CE
Muhammad born in the wilds of Saudi Arabia — an illiterate caravan manager. Orphaned at a young age, raised by his uncle. An illiterate man from a trading city.
Pre-610
Married Khadija, a wealthy widow merchant — his only wife until her death. She was the first Muslim convert.
610 CE
"Night of Power" (Laylat al-Qadr) — Age 40, meditating in a cave on Mt. Hira, called by the angel Jibril (Gabriel) to be a prophet of Allah. Received revelations for 22 years. As is the case with most prophets, was really mistreated. He proclaimed sweeping reform from business to life, gender, and family relations.
619 CE
Year of Sorrow — the ummah is persecuted; his uncle (and protector) bankrupt and dies; Khadija dies; assassination attempts against Muhammad.
619–620 CE
The Night Journey & Ascension (Isra and Mi'raj) — While sleeping near the Ka'ba, Muhammad was awakened by Jibril, who mounted him on a mystical steed (Buraq) that flew him to Jerusalem (to the al-Masjid al-Aqsa / Furthest Mosque — the Temple Mount, site of the 2nd Jewish Temple). He climbed a ladder to the throne of God. He met great prophets: Abraham, Moses, Jesus, John the Baptist and others. He received guidance — including fixing the daily prayers at 5. This made Jerusalem the third holiest city after Mecca and Medina, and affirmed Islam as part of the Abrahamic lineage.
622 CE
The Hijra — Due to conflict in Mecca, the ummah flees to Medina. This migration is so significant the Muslim calendar begins with the year of the Hijra. Hijra or Jihad: migrate OR stay and resist — twin ideals and guiding principles (Greater Jihad = inner struggle; Lesser Jihad = outside struggles).
630 CE
The Meccan tribes recognized his leadership; feud came to an end. After tribes clashed and truce was broken, Muhammad moved against Mecca with a large force. He proved magnanimous in victory — used diplomacy rather than force. Many former enemies converted and became leaders. He regarded the Ka'ba as the true house of monotheism.
632 CE
Death of Muhammad. Islam split over succession: Sunni vs Shia.
⚠ IB Key — Islam & Earlier Religions

Islam and the Quran were intended to be a perfecting of Judaism and Christianity — NOT a replacement. Both were seen to have strayed from God's intention. Islam affirms the Torah and Gospels as originally revealed, but believes they were corrupted by human hands. The Quran is the final, uncorrupted revelation. This is why Muslims accept Abraham, Moses, and Jesus as prophets.

Sunni vs Shia & Other Groups

The Succession Split

Islam split soon after Muhammad's death (632 CE) over the question: who should lead the ummah?

Sunni — 80% of worldwide Muslims
  • Follows Abu Bakr as the 1st Rightly Guided Caliph
  • The majority of the ummah supported Abu Bakr — felt he was the most capable; he claimed the leadership
  • Sunni have not split into major sub-sects
  • Follow the Sunnah of the Prophet strictly
Shia — 20% of worldwide Muslims
  • Follows Ali (Muhammad's cousin and son-in-law) as the 4th Rightly Guided Caliph
  • Felt Ali should have led from the beginning — bloodline succession
  • Has several sub-sects: Fiver, Sevener, Twelver Shiites (based on how many Imams they recognise)
  • Shia sects have beliefs/doctrine that diverge from Sunni

Sufism

Islamic mysticism — associated with Shia tradition, sort of like monks. Responsible for much of the spread of Islam through personal devotion and mystical practice. Often rejected by Sunni as innovation. Features the Whirling Dervishes (a Sufi practice).

Nation of Islam

Founded in 1930 in Detroit by Wallace Fard Muhammad. An African-American religious movement that uses Islamic themes. Controversial — not recognized as orthodox Islam by mainstream Sunni or Shia.

What Sunni and Shia Agree On

  • Both agree on Muhammad and his status as the final Prophet
  • The Quran is sacred and irrefutable
  • The Five Pillars, including Hajj
⚠ IB Tip — Sunni/Shia Split

The Sunni/Shia split is fundamentally about political succession, not theology. They agree on core doctrine (Tawhid, Quran, Five Pillars) — the split is about who had the legitimate right to lead the ummah after Muhammad's death. Compare to the Christian Great Schism — also political as much as theological.

Sacred Texts, Sharia & Authority

The Quran

  • Sacred and irrefutable — the direct Word of God revealed to Muhammad by the angel Jibril (Gabriel) in Arabic over 22 years
  • Collated after Muhammad's death by the ummah under Caliph Uthman
  • Hafiz: a person who has memorized and can recite the entire Quran — MILLIONS have done this
  • Arabic is the sacred language — prayers are all in Arabic; the Quran "should" be read in Arabic
  • The Quran is meant to perfect earlier Abrahamic scriptures, NOT abrogate them

Sunnah & Hadith

  • Sunnah: the behaviour and actions of the Prophet — not sacred but very important as a guide to right behaviour (orthopraxy)
  • Hadith: a collection of sayings from the Prophet Muhammad — his advice, commentary, guidance to the ummah
  • Isnad: the documented/transmitted chain of knowledge from one scholar to the next, from Muhammad to the present — used to authenticate Hadith
  • Authentication of Hadith is an issue — people have claimed false hadith (e.g. the 72 virgins claim for terrorists; Muhammad's marriage age to Aisha)

Sources of Shariah (Islamic Law)

1
The Quran — primary and most authoritative source
2
Sunnah / Hadith — the Prophet's example
3
Qiyas — analogical reasoning (Fiqh): applying known rulings to new situations
4
Ijtihad — independent judgement/reasoning by qualified scholars
5
Ijma — consensus of the Ulama (scholars)

Key Roles & Institutions

  • Imam: local leaders at the masjid (mosque), like a rabbi — guides the community in prayer and Islamic life
  • Ulama: Islamic scholars who interpret and apply Islamic law
  • Qadi: Islamic judge who rules on legal matters
  • Islamic law schools (madhabs): Hanafi, Shafi'i, Maliki, Hanbali — each school varies in some rulings, some very conservative

Two Categories of Shariah Duties

  • Duties to God (Ibadah) — prayer, fasting, pilgrimage, etc.
  • Duties to others (Mu'amalat) — social, family, financial obligations
⚠ IB Key — Quran vs Hadith Authority

The Quran is the direct, uncorrupted Word of God — absolute authority. The Hadith are important guides but can be authenticated or rejected (the Isnad chain system). This hierarchy of authority matters for understanding how Islamic law works and why there is diversity within Islam.

Eschatology & The Quranic Universe

Three Levels of the Universe

The Quranic universe has three levels: Earth, Heaven, Hell. On Earth there are two types of beings: humans and spirits. All beings are called to obedience to God.

Spiritual Beings

  • Angels — messengers from God to human beings; immortal and genderless; function as guardians
  • Jinns (Genies) — invisible by nature but can appear in human form; made from fire. Can be good or bad. They too will be judged in the afterlife. Often portrayed as magical beings in folklore.
  • Shaytan / Iblis (Satan) — leader of the devils; a jinn who tempts humans in their jihad. Represents evil/disobedience to God. His fall was caused by his refusal to prostrate before Adam upon God's commandment — he was too proud.
  • Devils — fallen angels and jinns who tempt humans. The Fall represents the ongoing human struggle between good and evil.

Sin — No Original Sin

  • No Original Sin in Islam — humans are born in fitra (pure natural state)
  • Humans enjoy a special status because God breathed His spirit into the first being, Adam
  • Sin = the result of an act of disobedience, NOT a state of being
  • There is no need for guilt, shame or disgrace — just return to the straight path
  • The focus is on Jihad — the ongoing struggle for what is right and just in the path of God

The End Times

  • Barzakh — souls are with Allah between death and Resurrection Day
  • Yawm al-din (Judgement Day / Resurrection Day) — all souls reunite with their bodies; Allah has a book of deeds for every human
  • Jesus (Isa) will appear with Muhammad (pbuh) on the Day of Judgement
  • Jesus (Isa) will kill the Dajjal ("Fake Jesus" / the Antichrist)
  • Allah can/will send some souls to Jahannam for a while then bring to Janna — it is not eternal for all

Islamic View of Jesus (Isa)

  • Jesus is a great prophet — NOT the Son of God (that would be Shirk)
  • Jesus did NOT die on the cross — Allah placed a substitute on the cross; Jesus was taken to Heaven
  • Jesus will return at the end of times — a future role, not a past saving act
⚠ IB Key — Islam's View of Jesus

Islam affirms Jesus as a great prophet (Isa) but firmly denies his divinity and crucifixion. "Allah placed a substitute on the cross" — Jesus was taken directly to Heaven. This is a major IB comparison point between Islam and Christianity. Know this clearly.

The Five Pillars of Islam
Pillar 1
Shahadah — Declaration of Faith
"There is no God but God (Allah) and Muhammad is his Prophet." The foundational statement of Islamic faith. To become Muslim: recite with Niyya (sincere intention).
Pillar 2
Salat — Prayer (5 Times Daily)
5 times a day facing Mecca. Can be done anywhere there is a clean space. Group prayer is better than praying alone. Jummah (Friday communal prayer) required for men if not working. Muezzin calls from the minaret. Women not required to attend mosque.
Pillar 3
Zakat — Charity
Help those in need as much as possible. Obligatory giving — typically 2.5% of savings per year. Not optional generosity but a justice obligation (compare to Jewish Tzedakah).
Pillar 4
Sawm — Fasting (Ramadan)
Fasting during the holy month of Ramadan. No food, drink, smoking, or sex from dawn to sunset. During menstruation: no fasting (make up the days later), no praying, cannot handle the Quran, cannot enter the mosque.
Pillar 5
Hajj — Pilgrimage to Mecca
At least once in a lifetime ($$$). Only Muslims are allowed in Mecca. 10 days in the final month of the Islamic calendar (~2 months after Ramadan). Umrah = pilgrimage to Mecca at any time but does not count for Hajj.
⚠ IB Key — The Five Pillars

The Five Pillars are the obligatory framework of Muslim life prescribed in the Quran. They are the "how do we get there?" answer — the practical path of submission to God's will. Note: both Sunni and Shia agree on the Five Pillars. The Pillars make Islam a remarkably unified practice across 1.8 billion Muslims worldwide.

Holy Days, Worship & Halal/Haram

Worship & the Masjid (Mosque)

  • 5 daily prayers — can be done anywhere with a clean space
  • Jummah — Friday prayer at the masjid; required for men if not working
  • Muezzin — man who calls Muslims to prayer from the minaret of the mosque
  • Women are NOT required to go to the masjid at all
  • Men sit on the ground floor; women up in a balcony — separate but equal in theory
  • Menstruation: cannot enter mosque, no fasting (make up the days), no praying, don't handle the Quran
Ramadan
Holy month of fasting — the month the Quran was first revealed. Fast from dawn to sunset. Night prayers (Tarawih). Community spirit intensifies. Laylat al-Qadr (Night of Power) — the night Muhammad first received revelation — falls near the end of Ramadan.
Eid al-Fitr
3-day festival to END Ramadan. Very festive — new clothes, gifts, communal prayers, special foods. Celebrated worldwide, not just in Muslim-majority countries.
Hajj (Pilgrimage)
10 days in the final month of the Islamic calendar (~2 months after Ramadan). Only Muslims allowed in Mecca. At least once in a lifetime. The largest annual peaceful gathering of humans on earth.
Eid al-Adha
Feast of the Sacrifice — end of Hajj. Commemorates Abraham's obedience to God in his willingness to sacrifice Ishmael (NOT Isaac — Islamic tradition differs from Jewish/Christian). An animal is ritually sacrificed, prepared, and distributed to the poor. Celebrated worldwide.
Ashura
Mainly a Shia holiday about the death of Muhammad's grandson Hussayn — commemorating his martyrdom at Karbala. Also observed by some Sunni as a day of fasting (Moses' crossing of the Red Sea).
Umrah
Pilgrimage to Mecca at any time of year — but does NOT count as Hajj. A voluntary act of devotion, not one of the Five Pillars.

Halal vs Haram

  • Halal (permitted): certain foods, slaughter of meat according to Islamic method, cats
  • Haram (prohibited): alcohol, pork, pre-marital relations, dating, breaking the Ten Commandments, dogs, charging interest on loans (riba)
  • Islam has its own banking/financial system — no interest (riba)
  • What is considered haram varies by culture: dancing, music, gold jewelry for men
⚠ IB Tip — Eid al-Adha

In Islamic tradition, it was Ishmael (not Isaac) whom Abraham was commanded to sacrifice. This is a key difference from the Jewish and Christian account. It also explains why Islam traces its religious lineage through Ishmael (ancestor of the Arabs) rather than Isaac (ancestor of the Jews). Know this for cross-religion comparison questions.

Women in Islam, Marriage & Religious Experience

The Quran Raised the Status of Women

In 7th-century Arabia, the revelation of Islam raised women's status by prohibiting:

  • Female infanticide (killing baby girls)
  • Treating women as property

And by granting women:

  • The right to receive and own their own dowry
  • Changing marriage from a proprietary to a contractual relationship
  • Allowing women to retain control over their property

Marriage (Nikah)

  • No dating in Islam; no unsupervised contact between genders
  • Wali — a woman's male guardian in marriage negotiations
  • Nikah — the religious ceremony/union in front of two witnesses; often done before a larger wedding reception
  • Type of wedding varies by region/culture
  • Women are to receive financial maintenance from husbands in marriage and/or divorce; men are complete providers for the family
  • Divorce: a man must say he wants it 3 times, breaking the religious union (not the civil marriage). Women can also pursue divorce.

The Hijab & Dress

  • The hijab or head/body coverings: NOT "required" (except in Afghanistan, maybe Iran) — the Quran says to dress modestly but nothing about a specific type of head covering
  • It is mostly a cultural/family thing — a controversial misconception that it is doctrinally mandated
  • Many Muslim women CHOOSE to wear some form of head covering
  • It should NOT be a symbol of repression or patriarchy

Equality vs Difference

The Quran declares that men and women are equal in the eyes of God — both created to be equal pairs. BUT: men and women are not equal in role — they have different rights and responsibilities due to their different natures. Regional patriarchy pre-dates Islam and has impacted women's rights in some countries.

Reversion, NOT Conversion

In Islamic theology, all humans are born in a state of purity (fitra) as Muslims — innately we have Imaan (faith in God). If raised outside of Islam, one does not "convert" but rather reverts to their original state at birth. To revert: recite the Shahadah with Niyya (sincere intention).

⚠ IB Key — Reversion

"Reversion not Conversion" — Islam believes all humans are born Muslim (in submission to God). Becoming Muslim is returning to one's true nature, not adopting something foreign. Compare to Christian conversion (which requires faith in Jesus as new belief) and Jewish conversion (which is actively discouraged). This theological difference reflects fundamentally different understandings of human nature.

Ethical & Legal Issues
  • Abortion: acceptable ONLY when it is for the health and safety of the mother
  • Circumcision: required for male infants
  • Birth control: varies but overall accepted; family planning is OK
  • IVF: OK if it's the married couple's own sperm and egg. No donor eggs. Surrogacy not really accepted but depends.
  • Homosexuality: No acceptance of anything — not marriage, not openly gay couples. Nothing at all. (Your notes say "Nada. Period.")
  • Riba (interest): Haram — Islam has its own banking system to avoid charging interest on loans
  • Alcohol: Haram
  • Pork: Haram

Discuss the significance of Tawhid (the oneness of God) as the foundational doctrine of Islam. Paper 2
Expand ▼

Key Argument

Tawhid is not merely one doctrine among many — it is the lens through which all of Islamic theology, ethics, law, and practice is understood. Everything in Islam flows from the absolute oneness of God.

Introduction Points

  • Define Tawhid: the absolute, uncompromising oneness of Allah — no partners, no Trinity, no Son
  • Shirk (associating partners with Allah) is the gravest sin — this shows how central Tawhid is
  • The Shahadah (1st Pillar) begins with Tawhid: "There is no God but God"

Body — How Tawhid Shapes Everything

  • Theology: Rejects the Christian Trinity as Shirk. Affirms Judaism's monotheism as closer to Islam but sees it as incomplete without Muhammad's revelation. Allah is utterly transcendent — no images, no physical depictions.
  • Ethics: All human beings are equal before the one God — no racial or class hierarchy. Zakaat (charity) flows from the equality all humans share under Tawhid.
  • Worship: All Five Pillars are directed exclusively at the one Allah. Prayer 5 times daily facing Mecca — the direction unifies the ummah toward one God. No intercessors, no saints (contrast with Catholicism).
  • Law (Shariah): The Quran is Allah's direct speech — its authority derives from Tawhid. No human law can override God's law.
  • Community: The ummah (global Muslim community) is united by shared submission to the one God — regardless of ethnicity, language, or culture.

Counter-argument

  • Some argue the Five Pillars are more "foundational" in practice — they are what Muslims actually do daily. Tawhid is belief; the Pillars are action.
