⚡

IB Exam Tip: Jainism is often compared to Sanatana Dharma in Paper 2. Key contrasts: Jainism rejects the Vedas, the caste system, and the need for god(s). Both share belief in karma, samsara, ahimsa, and moksha — but their definitions and paths differ significantly.

★

Essential Test Facts

▼
Jainism is described as a "heresy" of Hinduism — it denied the validity of the Vedas as inspired scripture and rejected the caste system.
Jainism does not believe in the need for god(s). God(s) are liberated souls — perfect beings who have destroyed all karma. There is no creator, preserver, or destroyer god. Jainism is non-theistic.
Prayer and worship are considered useless in Jainism — only YOU alone can help you attain Moksha. You should focus on how to be a Jina.
The ultimate goal of Jains is to become a Jina — one who has conquered the forces of life (karma, ego, greed, deceit, anger).
Jainism's cosmology: over very long periods of time, truth is discovered, fades, and is lost, and is found again. The universe has no beginning or ending.
🏛

Origins & Context

▼

Jainism as a "Heresy" of Sanatana Dharma

  • Rejected the Vedas as divinely inspired scripture
  • Rejected the religious implications of the caste system
  • Rejected the need for god(s)
  • Yet shares key concepts with Sanatana Dharma: karma, samsara, ahimsa, moksha — with very different definitions

Axial Age Context

Mahavira (599–527 BCE) was a contemporary of Siddhartha (the Buddha), Confucius, Lao-tzu, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, and the Greek philosophers — all part of the "Axial Age," a remarkable period of simultaneous spiritual awakening worldwide.

👑

The Life of Mahavira

▼
  • Dates: 599–527 BCE, or died as late as 467 BCE
  • Background: Kshatriyan prince — similar to the story of the historical Buddha (Siddhartha): wealthy background, gave it all up to become an extreme ascetic
  • Orthodox Jains believe Mahavira was the 24th Tirthankara — the last in a long line of 24 founders. The 23 before him were also Tirthankaras.
  • A Tirthankara = "bridge builder" — one who forges a bridge between life and moksha
  • He taught that one way to achieve release is to practice ahimsa
  • He avoided all attachment to people and things
  • Purposely tortured himself and after 12 years attained kevala (pure consciousness)
  • Became known as the Jina — the conqueror — because he came to know his true self
  • Lived for another 30 years after achieving kevala
  • Preached in local dialects (Prakrit) rather than Sanskrit — this helped attract followers AND rejected Brahmin control of Sanskrit rituals and the caste system
  • Also rejected the Brahmin caste system and their control of religious authority
Mahavira's choice to preach in Prakrit (not Sanskrit) was both a religious and political act — it democratized access to spiritual teaching by bypassing the Brahmin monopoly on religious language.
🔄

The Human Condition

▼

1. What is the Human Condition?

Jains believe humans are caught in samsara — the endless cycle of life, death, and rebirth — characterized by ignorance and suffering. The jiva (soul) is condemned to samsara and, as a result of collecting karma (material particles), is weighed down with ajiva (matter/flesh).

2. Where Are We Going? (Eschatology)

  • To a favorable or unfavorable rebirth after death (based on karma)
  • To Moksha — liberation of the jiva from the ajiva

3. How Do We Get There?

  • Following the teachings of Mahavira and the Five Great Vows, including ahimsa
  • Practicing asceticism
  • The purpose of giving up everything is to attain kevala — a pure, omniscient consciousness — which then leads to moksha
  • Practicing the 12 Great Vows (for monks and nuns)

Key Jain Concepts of the Soul

  • Jiva = the soul, the immortal essence — present in ALL living things, including insects, plants, bacteria, and viruses
  • Ajiva = not the soul; represents matter like the body
  • Karma in Jainism = actual physical particles attracted to the jiva by the jiva's actions (what we do, think, etc.). Karma is a MATERIAL pollution of the jiva — very different from Hindu karma.
  • The entire universe can be identified as either jiva or ajiva. Sentient beings are jiva encased in ajiva.
  • Souls can take on different physical forms — even rocks
  • Mahavira taught that karma is built up as a result of ANY activity — so the ideal life for a Jain might be to do as little as possible to escape karma
👥

The Majority vs. The Minority (Monks)

▼

The Majority — Lay Jains

  • Non-ascetic; live in society
  • "Cannot afford" to leave their homes and accept the rigors of the ascetic life
  • Follow 3 of the 5 great vows (Ahimsa, no lying, no stealing)
  • Strict vegetarians / vegans
  • Work in commercial business (they're known as very trustworthy)
  • Often very wealthy

The Minority — Monks and Nuns

  • Become ascetic, celibate monks/nuns
  • About 6,000 monks/nuns in India total
  • Jains of any age can take "dhiksa" (monastic initiation)
  • Take all 12 great vows
  • Since the way to release the jiva from the ajiva is through strict asceticism, the role of the monk/nun is necessary for attaining kevala and moksha
  • Cremation to purify the soul
  • Santhara (ritual suicide through fasting) is considered honorable — a final act of non-violence and detachment
🙏

The Five Anuvratas (Lesser Vows)

▼
The Anuvratas are the Lesser or Limited Vows — followed by the Majority (lay Jains). Monks follow the full/Great Vows (Mahavratas) which are stricter versions of the same five principles.

1. Ahimsa — Non-Violence

  • Jains must do their best to avoid any intentional hurt to living things
  • In daily life, harm can be minimized by filtering water, not eating at night, etc.
  • Intentional hurt includes cases of avoidable negligence
  • Jains must be vegetarians (at minimum)
  • Jains MAY use violence in self-defense or to protect someone from harm
  • If a Jain's work unavoidably causes harm (e.g. driving), they should minimize the harm, maintain complete detachment, and fast as penance

2. Satya — Truthfulness

  • Jains must always be truthful
  • Jains must always conduct business honestly
  • Dishonesty by NOT doing something is as bad as being actively dishonest

3. Asteya / Achaurya — Non-Stealing

  • Jains must not steal
  • Jains must not cheat
  • Jains must not avoid paying tax

4. Brahmacharya — Chastity

  • Jains must have sex only with the person they are married to
  • Jains must avoid sexual indulgence even with that person
  • Jains must give up sex, if possible, after the marriage has yielded a son

5. Aparigraha — Non-Possession

  • Jains must only possess what they need
  • Jains must live simply
  • Jains must not use surplus possessions to benefit others
  • Jains must not use too many resources
🥗

Dietary Restrictions (Ahimsa in Practice)

▼
All dietary restrictions flow from ahimsa — avoiding harm to living things. Every food restriction has a reason related to protecting jivas.
  • No root vegetables — digging up a root kills the plant (a living jiva)
  • No eating after dark — lack of sunlight causes bacteria to flourish; also risk of accidentally eating insects
  • No storing cooked food — bacteria grow in stored food
  • Plants must be harvested in a way that does not hurt the plant
  • Dairy only if locally sourced
  • Honey is forbidden — harmful to bees
  • Mushrooms and yeast are forbidden — may contain parasites and other organisms
  • Onions, eggplants, and garlic are off limits — they produce lethargy, a putrid smell, and create sexual energy (counter to brahmacharya)
  • At minimum: strict vegetarians. Many are vegan.
💎

The Triple Gems of Jainism

▼

Together, the Triple Gems provide the "bridge" for attaining liberation from the cycles of birth and death. They must all go together — one cannot exist without the other two.

1. Right Vision / Faith — Samyak Darshana

Faith in the Tirthankaras; seeing the Truth clearly; distinguishing Jiva from Ajiva

2. Right Knowledge — Samyak Jnana

Complete understanding of Jain principles; deep comprehension of the self, the universe, and karma

3. Right Conduct — Samyak Charitra

Applies the other two gems to provide an ethical guideline — i.e., following the 5 great vows

The Triple Gems are sometimes compared to the Buddhist Eightfold Path and the Hindu Trivarga. In Jainism, Right Conduct = the Anuvratas. All three gems must be present simultaneously for liberation to be possible.
📜

Sacred Texts — The Agamas

▼
  • The Agamas are Jain scripture / holy writings
  • Orthodox Jains believe the Agamas are actual sermons or teachings that Mahavira gave to his disciples
  • There is debate as to how many are genuine and authoritative
  • Not all have been translated
  • The Svetambara and Digambara sects disagree on which Agamas are authoritative
Unlike the Hindu Vedas (Shruti — divinely revealed), the Agamas are understood as the recorded teachings of a human being (Mahavira) — reflecting Jainism's rejection of divine authorship.
⚖️

Jain Sects — Svetambara & Digambara

▼

By 80 BCE, Jains were divided over what was the true meaning of Jainism — they split into two main sects.

Svetambara — "White Clad"

  • Based in northern India
  • More liberal in their interpretations of Mahavira's teachings
  • White robes show they reject nudity as a requirement
  • Women ARE allowed into their religion, into monasteries, and they accept that women can find release / attain kevala/moksha
  • More popular of the two sects
  • Tirthankaras in temples are very elaborate and decorated

Digambara — "Sky Clad"

  • Based mainly in southern India
  • More conservative
  • Total nudists are the most holy — to men (sky-clad monks practice full nudity)
  • Women are seen as the greatest temptation and can NEVER achieve moksha
  • Tirthankaras in temples are unadorned and undecorated
The key split between Svetambara and Digambara is primarily over: (1) the role of women, (2) the requirement of nudity, and (3) interpretation of Mahavira's teachings.
🎉

Holy Days

▼

Paijusana (Paryushana)

The main Jain holiday — Jains are encouraged to fast and live as monks during this period. A time of intense reflection, confession, and spiritual renewal.

Diwali

Jains celebrate Diwali as the liberation (moksha) of Mahavira — the day he achieved final release. Unlike the Hindu celebration (which honors Lakshmi and the return of Rama), Jain Diwali is specifically about Mahavira's liberation.

"Examine the role of ahimsa in Jainism, with reference to both belief and practice."
▼
1

Introduction

  • Define ahimsa: non-violence to all living things
  • Contextualise: ahimsa is the supreme principle of Jainism, flowing directly from the belief that ALL living things contain a jiva (soul)
  • Thesis: ahimsa is not merely an ethical rule but the foundational expression of Jain cosmology and soteriology (path to liberation)
2

Theological Basis for Ahimsa

  • All living things — including insects, plants, bacteria, and viruses — contain a jiva (soul)
  • Harming any jiva generates bad karma (actual physical particles) that attach to the soul and deepen its bondage in samsara
  • Therefore, ahimsa is not just compassion — it is the primary way to avoid accumulating karma
  • Mahavira's teaching: the ideal life might be to do as little as possible to avoid generating karma
3

Ahimsa in Daily Practice (Majority / Lay Jains)

  • Strict vegetarianism / veganism
  • Extensive dietary restrictions: no root vegetables (kills plant), no eating after dark (bacteria/insects), no honey, no garlic/onions/eggplant, no mushrooms/yeast
  • Filtering water before drinking
  • Sweeping the path before walking (to avoid stepping on insects)
  • Avoiding occupations that cause harm
4

Ahimsa in Monastic Practice (Minority / Monks)

  • The 1st and most important of the 5 Great Vows (Mahavratas)
  • Even more extreme restrictions than lay Jains
  • Santhara (ritual fasting unto death) is the ultimate act of ahimsa — dying without harming anyone
  • Jain monks sweep the ground before them and filter water
5

Ahimsa & the Triple Gems

  • Ahimsa is the foundation of Right Conduct (Samyak Charitra) — the 3rd Triple Gem
  • Without Right Conduct (ahimsa), the other two gems (faith and knowledge) cannot produce liberation
  • Ahimsa links belief (jiva cosmology) to practice (the vows) — it is the bridge between Jain theology and Jain ethics
6

Conclusion

  • Ahimsa is the supreme ethical principle of Jainism and flows directly from core Jain cosmology (jiva in all things)
  • It governs diet, occupation, speech, thought, and even the manner of death
  • Without ahimsa, liberation (moksha) is impossible
Key Terms: ahimsa, jiva, ajiva, karma, samsara, moksha, kevala, Anuvratas, Mahavratas, Triple Gems, Samyak Charitra, Santhara, Mahavira, Tirthankaras
"To what extent is Jainism best understood as a reaction to Sanatana Dharma rather than an independent religion?"
▼
1

Introduction

  • Jainism has been called a "heresy" of Hinduism — it explicitly rejected key Hindu institutions
  • Yet it shares fundamental concepts: karma, samsara, moksha, ahimsa, asceticism
  • Thesis: Jainism is best understood as both a reaction to Sanatana Dharma AND an independent tradition with genuinely distinct theology
2

Jainism AS a Reaction (Rejection of Sanatana Dharma)

  • Rejected the Vedas as divinely inspired — preached in Prakrit not Sanskrit, bypassing Brahmin authority
  • Rejected the caste system entirely
  • Rejected the need for god(s) — non-theistic; prayer is useless
  • Rejected Vedic animal sacrifice — extreme ahimsa as the counter-principle
  • Mahavira deliberately preached to all castes — a democratic spiritual democratization
3

Jainism's INDEPENDENT Theology

  • Karma as material particles (not a moral force) — a distinctly Jain doctrine
  • Jiva does NOT merge with Brahman at moksha — remains individual and conscious (vs. Hindu Atman=Brahman)
  • Non-theistic cosmology: universe has no beginning or end, no creator god
  • The role of the Tirthankara is unique — 24 bridge-builders, not avatars of a deity
  • Triple Gems as the path — no Bhakti (devotion to god) route to liberation
4

Shared Concepts (Challenge to the "Reaction" Thesis)

  • Both use the terms karma, samsara, moksha, ahimsa — but with different meanings
  • Both emerged from the same cultural milieu (Indian subcontinent, Axial Age)
  • This may reflect shared cultural origins rather than conscious borrowing
5

Conclusion

  • Jainism is partially a reaction — its explicit rejections of Vedic authority, caste, and theism are clear responses to Sanatana Dharma
  • But its unique cosmology (jiva/ajiva, material karma, non-theism, independent soul at moksha) constitutes a genuinely independent theological system
  • It is better described as emerging in dialogue with — not merely against — Sanatana Dharma
Key Terms: Jiva, Ajiva, karma, samsara, moksha, Tirthankaras, Mahavira, Agamas, ahimsa, Vedas, caste, Brahmin, Prakrit, Sanskrit, Triple Gems, henotheism, non-theism
"The Five Anuvratas are the most important element of Jain practice." How far do you agree?
▼
1

Introduction

  • The Five Anuvratas (Lesser Vows) are the ethical code for lay Jains: ahimsa, satya, asteya, brahmacharya, aparigraha
  • Thesis: the Anuvratas are central to Jain practice, but must be understood alongside the Triple Gems — Right Conduct cannot exist without Right Faith and Right Knowledge
2

Case FOR (Anuvratas as most important)

  • They are the practical application of all Jain belief — turning cosmology into daily life
  • Ahimsa (1st vow) is the supreme principle governing diet, occupation, speech, and thought
  • The Anuvratas govern the most visible, externally observable aspects of Jain identity
  • Even the Majority (lay Jains) who cannot follow the monastic path follow 3 of these vows
  • All 5 directly address the accumulation of karma — the fundamental problem in Jainism
3

Case AGAINST / Qualifications

  • Per the Triple Gems, Right Conduct (Anuvratas) cannot function without Right Faith and Right Knowledge — they are only one of three essential elements
  • For the Minority (monks), the Mahavratas (stricter vows) + full asceticism are more important than the Anuvratas
  • Asceticism broadly, not just the 5 vows, is essential for liberation
  • The Agamas (scriptures) provide the foundational knowledge that makes the vows meaningful — without right knowledge, vows are empty
4

Conclusion

  • The Anuvratas are the most practically important element for lay Jains — the daily expression of Jain faith
  • But theologically, they are the third of three interdependent Triple Gems — inseparable from Right Faith and Right Knowledge
  • "Most important" depends on your perspective: for practical daily life, yes; for liberation itself, no single element is sufficient alone
Key Terms: Anuvratas, Mahavratas, ahimsa, satya, asteya, brahmacharya, aparigraha, Triple Gems, Samyak Charitra, Samyak Darshana, Samyak Jnana, karma, jiva, moksha, kevala, asceticism
"Examine the role of the Tirthankaras in Jain belief and practice."
▼
1

Introduction

  • Define Tirthankara: "bridge builder" — a rare individual who has conquered samsara and leads others to liberation
  • 24 Tirthankaras in the current cosmic cycle; Mahavira is the 24th and last
  • Thesis: Tirthankaras are venerated teachers and models of liberation — not gods — reflecting Jainism's non-theistic character
2

Who are the Tirthankaras?

  • 24 spiritual teachers across the current cosmic cycle — not gods, but liberated human beings
  • Mahavira (24th) is the most recent and most significant — a Kshatriyan prince who gave up wealth and became an extreme ascetic
  • Contemporary of the Buddha, Confucius, Lao-tzu — part of the Axial Age
  • After 12 years of self-torture, achieved kevala (pure consciousness)
  • Became the Jina — "the conqueror" of karma, ego, greed, deceit, and anger
  • Preached for 30 more years in Prakrit, attracting all castes
3

Role in Jain Theology

  • First Triple Gem: Right Faith (Samyak Darshana) = faith in the Tirthankaras
  • They are the source of Right Knowledge — the Agamas are their recorded teachings
  • They are models of liberation, not intercessors — Jains cannot pray to them for help (since they are beyond the world)
  • Venerated, not worshipped — their lives inspire others to pursue the same path
4

Tirthankaras in Practice — Sect Differences

  • Svetambara: Tirthankaras depicted elaborately and decorated in temples
  • Digambara: Tirthankaras depicted unadorned and naked — emphasizing detachment from all material things
  • Paijusana and Diwali both reflect reverence for Mahavira's spiritual achievements
5

Conclusion

  • Tirthankaras are central to Jainism as models, teachers, and the source of Jain scripture (Agamas)
  • Their veneration is distinct from worship — reflecting Jainism's non-theistic character
  • The goal of every Jain is ultimately to become a Jina themselves
Key Terms: Tirthankara, Mahavira, Jina, kevala, moksha, Agamas, Triple Gems, Samyak Darshana, Svetambara, Digambara, non-theistic, asceticism, Axial Age, Prakrit
"Discuss the concept of karma in Jainism, comparing it to the concept of karma in Sanatana Dharma."
▼
1

Introduction

  • Both religions use the term "karma" but understand it in fundamentally different ways
  • Thesis: while both see karma as what binds the soul to samsara, Jainism understands karma as physical matter, whereas Sanatana Dharma understands it as a cosmic moral force
2

Karma in Jainism

  • Karma = actual material particles attracted to the jiva by the jiva's actions, thoughts, and speech
  • Karma is a physical pollution of the soul — it literally weighs the jiva down with ajiva (matter)
  • ANY activity generates karma — even involuntary actions
  • Karma is generated by: actions, thoughts, speech, and encouraging others to act harmfully
  • Removing karma requires asceticism, ahimsa, and the 5 vows — NOT devotion to a god
  • Santhara (fasting unto death) is the ultimate removal of karma
3

Karma in Sanatana Dharma

  • Karma = the law of action — a cosmic moral force; every action and thought has a consequence
  • Four types: Sanchita, Prarabdha, Kriyamana, Agami
  • Bad karma corrupts the universe, not just the individual
  • Karma determines varna (caste) — a social dimension absent in Jainism
  • Karma can be addressed through Bhakti (devotion to god) — a path unavailable in non-theistic Jainism
4

Key Differences

  • Jain karma is material/physical; Hindu karma is a cosmic moral law
  • Jain karma is generated by ANY activity; Hindu karma is generated by ethically significant choices
  • Jain karma removal = pure asceticism; Hindu karma removal = multiple paths including devotion
  • Jain karma has no social implications (no caste); Hindu karma determines one's birth into a caste
5

Conclusion

  • The shared term "karma" masks profound conceptual differences between the two traditions
  • Both agree karma is the fundamental problem binding souls to samsara
  • They disagree on its nature (material vs. moral), its scope (all activity vs. ethical choice), and its solution (asceticism alone vs. multiple paths)
Key Terms: karma, jiva, ajiva, samsara, moksha, kevala, ahimsa, Anuvratas, asceticism, Santhara, Atman, Brahman, varna, Bhakti, Sanchita karma, Prarabdha karma, Three Yogas/Margas

Q1(a) [2 marks]
Identify TWO practices described in the source and explain the principle that underlies them both.
The two practices are: (1) sweeping the path with a feather broom to avoid harming insects, and (2) filtering water to avoid swallowing microscopic organisms. Both practices are expressions of ahimsa — non-violence — the supreme ethical principle of Jainism. These practices reflect the Jain belief that ALL living things — even the smallest organisms — contain a jiva (soul), and therefore must not be harmed. Any harm, even accidental, generates karma (material particles) that attach to the monk's own jiva and deepen his bondage in samsara.
Q1(b) [4 marks]
Using the source and your own knowledge, explain what the source reveals about the Jain understanding of karma.
The source reveals the distinctly Jain understanding of karma as a material substance — actual physical particles attracted to the jiva (soul) by the jiva's actions, thoughts, and speech. This is fundamentally different from the Hindu concept of karma as a cosmic moral law.

The monk's extreme precautions (sweeping the road, filtering water, eating only before noon, owning nothing) all reflect Mahavira's teaching that karma is generated by ANY form of activity — even involuntary or accidental harm. Since karma particles physically "weigh down" the soul (jiva) with matter (ajiva), the path to liberation requires minimizing all activity that generates karma.

The monk's non-possession (owning only a robe and broom) reflects the 5th Anuvrata — Aparigraha — since even possessing objects creates attachment and karma. His estrangement from family reflects the monastic requirement of detachment from all worldly bonds, which is the path of the Minority — strict ascetics who follow all 12 Great Vows (Mahavratas) in pursuit of kevala and moksha.
Q1(c) [6 marks]
Using the source and your own knowledge, analyze how the concepts of jiva and ajiva shape the beliefs and practices of Jainism.
The jiva/ajiva distinction is the foundational cosmological claim of Jainism and the source of all Jain ethical and religious practice.

1. Cosmological significance: The entire universe can be divided into jiva (soul — all sentient, conscious living things) and ajiva (non-soul — inert matter, the body). Sentient beings are jiva encased in ajiva. Souls can take on different physical forms — even rocks contain a jiva. This makes the universe a vast web of conscious souls, all worthy of protection.

2. Basis for Ahimsa: Because ALL living things — insects, plants, bacteria, and viruses — have a jiva, harming any living thing is an act of violence against a soul. This is why the monk in the source sweeps the road and filters his water: these are expressions of ahimsa, flowing directly from the jiva/ajiva cosmology.

3. Karma as jiva pollution: Karma in Jainism is understood as material particles (ajiva) that become attracted to the jiva through the actions of body, speech, and mind. The karma particles literally weigh the jiva down — pulling it further into the material world of samsara. The goal is to purify the jiva by removing all ajiva (karma) from it.

4. Moksha as separation: Liberation (moksha) in Jainism means the complete separation of the jiva from all ajiva — the soul rises to the top of the universe in a state of pure, omniscient consciousness (kevala). Unlike Hindu moksha (where Atman merges with Brahman), the liberated Jain jiva remains individual and distinct.

In conclusion, jiva and ajiva shape every aspect of Jainism — from its cosmology, to its supreme ethical principle (ahimsa), to its understanding of karma and liberation. The entire goal of Jain life is the liberation of the jiva from ajiva.

One group argued that the great teacher had always worn simple white robes, and that women, too, could achieve the highest state of spiritual liberation. The other group insisted that the teacher had gone about naked, possessing nothing — not even clothing — and that this was the only true path. Q2(a) [2 marks]
Identify the two groups described in the source and give the Sanskrit name for each.
The two groups are: (1) Svetambara ("white-clad") — the group that believes the teacher (Mahavira) wore white robes and that women can attain liberation (moksha/kevala); and (2) Digambara ("sky-clad") — the group that insists Mahavira was naked and that total nudity (for men) is essential to liberation, and that women cannot attain moksha in a female body. The split occurred by approximately 80 BCE.
Q2(b) [4 marks]
Using the source and your knowledge, explain the significance of nudity in Jain practice and why it is controversial.
For Digambara Jains, nudity (being "sky-clad") is the ultimate expression of Aparigraha (non-possession) — the 5th Anuvrata. Even clothing constitutes a form of material attachment (ajiva) that binds the jiva to the world. To possess nothing — not even clothes — is seen as the fullest realization of detachment and therefore essential to attaining kevala (pure consciousness) and eventually moksha.

The controversy arises from the Svetambara position that this interpretation is incorrect: they believe Mahavira wore white robes, and that nudity is therefore not required for liberation. Their white robes signify a rejection of nudity while maintaining simplicity and detachment.

The second major controversy is the role of women: Digambaras argue that women can never be fully naked in public (due to social norms) and therefore can never fully renounce all possessions — meaning they cannot attain moksha in a female body. Women are also seen as the greatest temptation to men seeking liberation. Svetambaras reject this, accepting women as monks/nuns who can attain liberation. This has significant implications for gender equality within Jainism.

Question 1 [30 marks]
"In Jainism, liberation is impossible without asceticism." To what extent do you agree?
Agree — asceticism IS essential:
• Mahavira's example: 12 years of self-torture → kevala → moksha. He modeled that the only path to liberation is complete physical renunciation
• Jain karma is generated by ANY activity — the less one does, the less karma one generates; ascetic inactivity is literally the only way to stop accumulating karma
• The Minority (monks/nuns) — only they have the full possibility of attaining kevala in this life; the Majority (lay Jains) can only accumulate good karma for a better rebirth
• Santhara (fasting unto death) = the ultimate ascetic act, the ultimate liberation from karma
• The Triple Gems require Right Conduct (Samyak Charitra) — the 12 Mahavratas — only fully achievable by monks

Disagree / Nuances:
• The Majority (lay Jains) follow 3 Anuvratas and contribute to liberation over many lifetimes — Jainism does not require everyone to be a monk immediately
• The Triple Gems require Right Faith AND Right Knowledge alongside Right Conduct — asceticism alone (without understanding) is insufficient
• Svetambara Jains accept that women can attain moksha — complicating the stricter ascetic model of Digambaras
• Jainism has a cosmological cycle — truth is found again and again — suggesting gradual progress over cosmic time, not necessarily immediate ascetic renunciation

Conclusion: Asceticism is the most direct and highest path in Jainism, but the tradition accommodates gradual progress for lay followers. The goal remains liberation; asceticism is the most efficient — but not the only — means of getting there.
Question 2 [30 marks]
"Jainism and Sanatana Dharma share the same ultimate goal but disagree on how to reach it." Evaluate this claim.
Same goal (agree):
• Both seek moksha — liberation from samsara (the cycle of rebirth)
• Both see karma and ignorance as the fundamental problem
• Both see the soul as ultimately pure and capable of liberation
• Both traditions value ahimsa and asceticism

Different goals (challenge):
• Hindu moksha = union/identity of Atman with Brahman (dissolution of individual self)
• Jain moksha = the jiva rises to the top of the universe as a pure, individual, omniscient consciousness (Jiva remains individual — does NOT merge)
• Hindu goal includes Bhakti (devotion to a personal god) as a path — Jainism rejects this entirely (non-theistic)
• Jain liberation (kevala) while alive is possible; Hindu moksha traditionally ends rebirth at death

Different paths:
• Sanatana Dharma: Three Margas (Bhakti, Jnana, Karma) — flexible, one path can suffice
• Jainism: Triple Gems must ALL be present simultaneously — no single path is sufficient; asceticism is central
• Sanatana Dharma allows devotion to god; Jainism considers prayer useless
• Sanatana Dharma preserves caste as spiritually meaningful; Jainism rejects caste entirely

Conclusion: The claim is partially correct — both seek escape from samsara — but the nature of liberation (individual vs. merged soul) and the paths are genuinely different. The apparent similarity conceals deep theological divergence.
Question 3 [30 marks]
"The concept of the Jina is the most important concept in Jainism." How far do you agree?
Agree — The Jina is central:
• "Jainism" = the religion of the Jinas. The word Jain derives from Jina — a conqueror of karma
• The Jina is the GOAL of every Jain — not to worship a Jina, but to become one
• The Tirthankaras (including Mahavira) are Jinas who serve as perfect models of liberation
• First Triple Gem (Right Faith) = faith in the Tirthankaras — the Jinas are the foundation of Jain belief
• Jainism's non-theism means there is no God to worship — the Jinas are the closest equivalent to divine figures

Other important concepts:
• Jiva/Ajiva: the foundational cosmological distinction — without this, nothing else in Jainism makes sense
• Karma (as material substance): the specific Jain understanding drives all Jain ethics and practice
• Ahimsa: the supreme ethical principle, flowing from jiva cosmology — arguably more operationally central than the Jina concept
• Triple Gems: the threefold path to liberation — without these, even understanding the Jina is insufficient

Conclusion: The concept of the Jina is central to Jainism's identity, goals, and name. But cosmologically, jiva/ajiva and karma may be more foundational — the Jina is the DESTINATION, but the jiva/ajiva/karma framework explains WHY that destination is necessary and how to reach it.
