Core Beliefs & Key Terms

The Shema — Most Important Prayer

"Shema Yisrael, Adonai Eloheinu, Adonai Echad" — "Hear, O Israel: the Lord is our God, the Lord is One." (Deuteronomy 6:4). This is the central declaration of Jewish faith — absolute monotheism.

⚠ IB Tip

The Shema is Judaism's most important prayer — not a commandment ritual, but a declaration of identity and belief. Know this for Paper 2.

⚠ IB Tip — Shekinah

Shekinah is the feminine grammatical form of God's presence in Hebrew — this does NOT mean Judaism believes in a female God. It represents God dwelling among the people. IB examiners love testing this nuance.

Human Condition & the Purpose of Life

Created in God's Image (B'tzelem Elohim)

Humans are created in the image of God (Genesis 1:27). This gives every human inherent dignity. It also means humans have free will — the constant choice between yetzer ha-ra and yetzer ha-tov.

The Constant Struggle

Unlike Christianity, Judaism does not have the concept of original sin as a permanent stain. Instead, humans are born morally neutral and must continuously choose between good and evil inclinations. Life is a battleground of moral choices.

Goal of Life

  • Live as a member of the House of Israel (the Jewish community/covenant people)
  • Observe the 613 mitzvot of the covenant
  • Pursue Tikkun Olam — repair and improve the world
  • Bring about the Messianic age of peace and justice
⚠ IB Critical Distinction

Judaism emphasises this-world observance over concern about the hereafter. Olam ha-ba exists but is vague. Compare to Christianity (Heaven/Hell very defined) and Buddhism (reincarnation cycle). IB Paper 2 comparative questions test this directly.

Chosen People — House of Israel

Jews believe they are "chosen" not in a supremacist sense, but as bearers of a covenant responsibility — entrusted with God's Torah to be a "light unto the nations." It is a burden of obligation, not superiority.

⚠ IB Key Point — Exile & Theological Evolution

The destruction of the First Temple (586 BCE) forced Judaism to evolve: God cannot be limited to a place. The synagogue replaces the Temple, the rabbi replaces the priest, prayer replaces sacrifice. This is huge for understanding Rabbinic Judaism.

Sacred Texts

TaNakh — The Hebrew Bible

Torah (תורה) + Nevi'im (נביאים) + Ketuvim (כתובים)

  • Torah — Five Books of Moses (Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, Deuteronomy). THE most sacred text. Contains the 613 mitzvot.
  • Nevi'im (Prophets) — Joshua, Judges, Isaiah, Jeremiah, Amos, etc.
  • Ketuvim (Writings) — Psalms, Proverbs, Job, Ecclesiastes, Ruth, Esther, etc.

Talmud — Oral Torah

The Talmud = Mishna (oral law compiled ~200 CE by Rabbi Judah ha-Nasi) + Gemara (rabbinic commentary on the Mishna). Two versions: Jerusalem Talmud and Babylonian Talmud (Babylonian is the authoritative one, finalised ~500 CE).

⚠ Key Metaphor to Remember

"Torah is the house; Talmud is the fence around the house." — The Talmud protects the Torah by creating boundaries around biblical law so you don't accidentally break it. This metaphor could appear in Paper 1 source analysis.

Shulchan Aruch

Compiled by Joseph Karo (1565 CE) — practical guide/code for following Halakhah based on the Talmud. Still used in Orthodox communities today.

Authority Hierarchy

Torah → Talmud → Shulchan Aruch → Responsa literature (rabbinic rulings on new questions)

Denominations & Diversity
Orthodox
  • All 613 mitzvot binding
  • Separate men/women worship (mechitza)
  • Only synagogue (not "Temple")
  • No electricity on Shabbat
  • No Bat Mitzvah (traditionally)
  • Does not accept homosexuality
  • Strict Kashrut
  • "Who is a Jew?" — through mother only
Reform (Liberal)
  • Haskalah (Enlightenment) 1800s — Germany
  • Ethical mitzvot binding; ritual optional
  • Use the term "Temple" (not just synagogue)
  • Accepts Bat Mitzvah, women rabbis
  • Accepts homosexuality
  • "Who is a Jew?" — mother OR father
  • Services partly in vernacular
Conservative
  • Middle ground between Orthodox and Reform
  • Halakhah binding but open to reinterpretation
  • Accepts Bat Mitzvah, women rabbis (more recently)
  • Stricter than Reform on ritual observance
Humanistic / Reconstructionist
  • Focus on social issues and cultural identity
  • Opposed to 613 laws as literally binding
  • Judaism as culture/civilization, not just religion

Ethnic Communities

  • Sephardim — Spanish & Portuguese Jews; expelled 1492; speak/spoke Ladino (Judeo-Spanish); settled N. Africa, Middle East, Americas
  • Ashkenazi — Eastern European & German Jews; speak/spoke Yiddish (Judeo-German); majority of world Jewry today
⚠ IB Tip — Who Is a Jew?

Judaism is simultaneously an ethnicity, religion, culture, and nation. "Who is a Jew?" is genuinely contested — Orthodox says maternal descent only; Reform says either parent. Conversion is possible but discouraged — must ask a rabbi 3 times, take ~1 year (Orthodox), and requires Mikvah (ritual bath) + Beit Din (rabbinic court). This complexity is unique among world religions.

The Diaspora & Key Institutions

Diaspora

After 70 CE (Roman destruction of the Temple), Jews dispersed across the world. The synagogue became the centre of Jewish communal life everywhere — worship, study, community gathering.

The Synagogue

  • Local centre of Jewish life — prayer, study, community
  • Minyan — quorum of 10 adult males required for communal prayer (Orthodox); some denominations count women too
  • Contains the Ark (Aron Kodesh) holding Torah scrolls
  • The Torah scroll is written by hand — sacred object

Key Roles

  • Rabbi — interpreter of Torah and Talmud; teacher and community leader (NOT a priest — there are no priests after 70 CE)
  • Cantor / Chazzan — leads musical/sung portions of worship
⚠ IB Tip — Rabbi vs. Priest

After the destruction of the Temple (70 CE), the priestly class (Kohanim) lost their primary function (animal sacrifice). The Rabbi — a scholar and teacher — replaced the priest as community leader. This is a key shift in Jewish history.

Rituals & Lifecycle Events

Birth

Brit Milah — circumcision on the 8th day for boys; sign of the Abrahamic covenant. Girls: naming ceremony (Simchat Bat in some traditions).

Coming of Age

Bar Mitzvah (boys, age 13) / Bat Mitzvah (girls, age 12–13 in non-Orthodox denominations) — "Son/Daughter of the Commandment." Child takes on full responsibility for mitzvot. Reads from the Torah scroll in synagogue.

Marriage

  • Ketubah — Jewish marriage contract outlining husband's obligations
  • Chuppah — wedding canopy; symbolises new home
  • Seven blessings recited (Sheva Brachot)
  • Breaking of a glass — remembrance of Temple destruction

Divorce

Get — Jewish bill of divorce; must be issued by the husband (Orthodox). A woman without a Get is an agunah (chained woman) — a modern controversy in Orthodox communities.

Death & Mourning

  • Body washed and prepared (Tahara); buried quickly (within 24 hours if possible)
  • No cremation in Orthodox tradition (body must return to earth)
  • Shiva — 7-day mourning period at home; community brings food
  • Yahrzeit (Ashkenazi) / Meldado (Sephardic) — anniversary candle lit on death anniversary
  • Kaddish prayer recited by mourners (does not mention death — praises God)
Festivals & Sacred Time
Shabbat
Most important observance. Friday sunset to Saturday nightfall. Commanded in the 10 Commandments (Exodus 20). Rest from all creative work; candle lighting, Kiddush, synagogue, family meal.
Rosh Hashanah
Jewish New Year. Month of Tishre (Sept/Oct). Shofar blown; apples and honey; Book of Life opened. 10 days of repentance begin.
Yom Kippur
Holiest day. Day of Atonement. 25-hour fast. No food, drink, bathing, leather shoes, marital relations. Book of Life sealed. Synagogue all day. Kol Nidre service.
Sukkot
Feast of Booths/Tabernacles. 7 days. Temporary outdoor huts (sukkah) — commemorates 40 years wandering in the desert. Four species: lulav, etrog, hadassim, aravot.
Simchat Torah
Immediately after Sukkot. Joyful celebration completing and restarting the annual Torah reading cycle. Torah scrolls paraded around synagogue.
Chanukah
MINOR festival (not in Torah). 8-day Feast of Lights. Celebrates rededication of Second Temple (165 BCE) after Maccabean revolt. Menorah/Chanukiyah lit. Latkes, dreidel.
Purim
Feast of Lots. Celebrates Queen Esther saving Jews from Haman in Persia (Book of Esther). Costumes, noisemakers (graggers), Megillah reading, gifts of food.
Pesach (Passover)
8 days (7 in Israel/Reform). Commemorates Exodus from Egypt. Seder meal: Haggadah read, 4 cups of wine, bitter herbs, matzah (unleavened bread). No chametz (leaven).
⚠ IB Critical Point — Chanukah

Chanukah is culturally prominent because it coincides with Christmas in Western culture — but it is a minor festival NOT commanded in the Torah. Never rank it as important as Yom Kippur or Shabbat in an IB answer. Examiner trap!

⚠ IB Tip — Shabbat

Shabbat is the MOST IMPORTANT observance because it is one of the Ten Commandments ("Remember the Sabbath day and keep it holy"). It happens every week, unlike annual festivals. Paper 2 often asks about the relationship between ritual and identity — Shabbat is the perfect example.

Kashrut — Dietary Laws

Kosher Principles

  • No pork (pig is not a kosher animal — must have split hooves AND chew cud)
  • No mixing dairy and meat — two separate sets of dishes, utensils, and cookware
  • Wait between eating meat and dairy (varies: 1–6 hours by denomination/custom)
  • Seafood — only fish with fins AND scales (no shellfish, shrimp, crab)
  • Kosher certification (hechsher) required on packaged goods
  • Animals must be slaughtered according to shechita (humane, swift cut; draining of blood)

Significance

Kashrut creates daily mindfulness of Jewish identity; it marks Jews as distinct. It is also an act of Kedushah (holiness). Non-observance of Kashrut is a key marker between Orthodox and Reform Jews.

⚠ IB Tip

Reform Judaism generally does not require Kashrut observance (it is a ritual, not ethical, mitzvah). For IB essays on denominations, Kashrut is an excellent specific example of where Orthodox and Reform diverge.

Eschatology & Ethics

Eschatology — The End of Things

  • No concrete Heaven/Hell (contrast Christianity and Islam)
  • Olam ha-ba — "World to Come" — vague; encompasses both the afterlife and the Messianic Age
  • Mashiach (Messiah) — a human leader (not divine) who will bring world peace, rebuild the Temple, and gather all Jews to Israel. Orthodox: still to come. Liberal streams: de-emphasise personal Messiah; focus on messianic era.
  • Reincarnation is an accepted belief in Kabbalistic tradition
  • Focus is THIS life — moral action, Tikkun Olam, study

Ethics — The 613 Mitzvot

  • 248 positive ("thou shalt") + 365 negative ("thou shalt not") = 613 total
  • Ethical mitzvot are binding in ALL denominations
  • Ritual mitzvot (dietary, dress, purity) — binding in Orthodox; optional in Reform
  • Pikuach Nefesh — saving a life overrides ALL other commandments except three (murder, sexual immorality, idolatry). Any mitzvah can be broken to save a life.
  • Tzedakah (charity/justice) — not optional; commanded giving of at least 10% of income
⚠ IB Key Concept — Pikuach Nefesh

The principle that saving a human life overrides almost any other Jewish law is fundamental to Jewish ethics. A doctor may work on Shabbat to save a life. This is a beautiful example of how Halakhah is applied dynamically — IB Paper 2 loves this for ethics questions.

Discuss the role of the Brit (covenant) in shaping Jewish identity and practice. Paper 2
Expand ▼

Key Argument

The Brit (covenant) is the foundational relationship between God and the Jewish people — it defines who Jews are, what they must do, and why it matters. Everything in Judaism flows from this covenantal relationship.

Introduction Points

  • Define Brit: a binding mutual agreement — God promises land, descendants, protection; Jews promise loyalty and observance of Torah
  • First established with Abraham (~1800 BCE); confirmed with Moses at Sinai (~1300 BCE)
  • The 613 mitzvot are the terms of the covenant

Body — Three Areas of Impact

  • Identity: Being Jewish = being part of the covenant community (House of Israel). The covenant applies to all generations — not just those at Sinai. "Chosen People" is covenantal responsibility, not supremacy.
  • Practice: Every mitzvah is a covenant act — Shabbat, Kashrut, Brit Milah (circumcision = physical sign of covenant), Bar/Bat Mitzvah (taking on covenant obligations)
  • Moral life: Tikkun Olam and Tzedakah as covenant obligations — repairing the world is part of the covenant deal. Teshuvah restores the covenant when broken.

Denominations Angle

  • Orthodox: covenant is unchanging and demands full 613-mitzvot observance
  • Reform: covenant is ethical at core; ritual expressions may evolve
  • Both agree the covenant is foundational — they disagree on what it requires

Conclusion

The Brit is not just a historical event — it is the living relationship that animates Jewish life. Every ritual, every ethical act, and every expression of identity is a covenant act. The Brit is what makes Judaism uniquely about collective responsibility before God.

To what extent does Tikkun Olam reflect the ethical priorities of Judaism? Paper 2
Expand ▼

Key Argument

Tikkun Olam ("repair the world") represents Judaism's this-worldly ethical focus — that God's purpose is not served by escaping the world but by actively improving it.

Introduction Points

  • Define Tikkun Olam: the ethical obligation to repair/improve the world through acts of justice (Tzedakah) and loving kindness (Gemilut Chasadim)
  • Rooted in the covenant: humans are co-creators with God in perfecting creation
  • Contrast with world-renouncing traditions (e.g., Buddhist withdrawal from samsara)

Body

  • This-world focus: Judaism is explicitly this-worldly. Olam ha-ba exists but is vague — Tikkun Olam is the here-and-now ethical imperative
  • Tzedakah: Not optional charity — the Hebrew root is "justice." Giving 10% (ma'aser) is commanded. Tikkun Olam = justice work, not just charity
  • Pikuach Nefesh: Saving a life overrides all other commandments — the supreme ethical principle. Life is sacred (B'tzelem Elohim — created in God's image)
  • Prophetic tradition: Amos, Isaiah, Micah — God demands justice, not ritual. "Let justice roll like waters" (Amos 5:24). Prophets established ethics as primary
  • Modern application: Reform Judaism especially emphasises Tikkun Olam — social justice movements (civil rights, environmentalism, poverty)

Counter-argument

  • Orthodox Judaism argues ritual observance (Kashrut, Shabbat, purity laws) is EQUALLY important — not just ethics. The 613 mitzvot include ritual as well as ethical
  • Some argue "Tikkun Olam" has been secularised — it originally referred to mystical restoration in Kabbalah, not social justice

Conclusion

Tikkun Olam reflects a core Jewish conviction: faith is measured by action in the world. While Orthodox Judaism insists ritual is equally essential, across denominations, ethics and justice are non-negotiable covenant obligations. Judaism's this-worldly focus sets it apart from traditions centred on escape from or transcendence of the world.

How has the authority of the Torah shaped Jewish belief and practice across different denominations? Paper 2
Expand ▼

Key Argument

The Torah is the uncontested centre of Jewish authority, but denominations differ profoundly on what that authority means and how it applies — this disagreement is the engine of Jewish denominational diversity.

Introduction Points

  • Torah = Five Books of Moses; contains the 613 mitzvot; most sacred text in Judaism
  • "Torah is the house; Talmud is the fence" — the protective role of rabbinic literature
  • The question is not WHETHER Torah is authoritative, but HOW it is authoritative

Body — Denominational Views

  • Orthodox: Torah was literally revealed by God at Sinai — the "divine dictation" view. Every word is authoritative. All 613 mitzvot binding. Halakhah is law, not guidance.
  • Conservative: Torah is divinely inspired but transmitted through human agency. Halakhah evolves but remains binding. Historical criticism acceptable.
  • Reform: Torah is the product of human beings seeking God — inspired, not dictated. Ethical commandments binding; ritual commandments are meaningful choices, not obligations. Torah must be reinterpreted for every generation.
  • Talmud as mediator: The Talmud shows that Torah has always been interpreted — the rabbis were constantly debating and reinterpreting. Even Orthodox Judaism is a tradition of interpretation.

Practical Consequences

  • Shabbat, Kashrut, family purity laws — observed strictly by Orthodox; modified or optional in Reform
  • Women's roles — Torah has no female rabbis; but its interpretation allows for it in liberal denominations
  • "Who is a Jew?" — divergent answers based on different readings of Torah and rabbinic tradition

Conclusion

The Torah's authority is absolute in principle across Judaism — but its meaning is hotly contested. From Orthodox to Reform, the spectrum of interpretation reflects centuries of rabbinic tradition wrestling with what God truly requires. This is not weakness but a strength: Judaism is a living, debating tradition.

Compare the understanding of life after death in Judaism and one other religion you have studied. Paper 2
Expand ▼

Key Argument

Judaism's eschatology is notably this-worldly and vague compared to Christianity's defined Heaven and Hell — this reflects Judaism's covenant focus on present action rather than future reward.

Judaism — Eschatology

  • No concrete Heaven/Hell; Olam ha-ba (World to Come) is intentionally vague in scripture
  • Mashiach — a human leader bringing a messianic AGE of peace, not an apocalyptic end
  • Reincarnation accepted in Kabbalistic tradition (gilgul neshamot)
  • Focus is firmly on THIS life: Tikkun Olam, observance of Torah, Teshuvah
  • The rabbis said: "Better one hour of repentance and good deeds in this world than all the life of the world to come"

Sikhism — Eschatology (comparison example)

  • Reincarnation cycle (samsara) until Mukti (liberation) — merger with Waheguru
  • Karma determines next rebirth; no Heaven/Hell as permanent states
  • Liberation through Naam Japna, Kirat Karni, Vand Chakna
  • Sikhism also focuses on THIS life — serving God through seva (selfless service)

Key Similarities

  • Both traditions focus more on THIS-WORLD action than hereafter
  • Both emphasise community service and ethical living as the path
  • Both have vague/non-punitive afterlife concepts compared to Christianity/Islam

Key Differences

  • Judaism: one life; no reincarnation (mainstream); Messiah brings historic peace age
  • Sikhism: multiple lives in reincarnation cycle; liberation = mystical union with God
  • Judaism: covenantal; Sikhism: devotional/meditational

Conclusion

Both Judaism and Sikhism are remarkable for their this-worldly focus — in contrast to the highly defined reward/punishment eschatologies of Christianity and Islam. However, Judaism's covenant framework makes eschatology secondary to faithful living in community, while Sikhism frames life as a spiritual journey toward divine union.

Key Argument

Unlike Christianity (centred on doctrine/belief in Jesus) or Islam (centred on the five pillars of faith and action), Judaism is unusually practice-centred — "orthopraxis" over "orthodoxy." But belief is not absent; it is embedded within practice.

Evidence FOR (practice-centred)

  • 613 mitzvot govern every area of daily life — what you eat, when you rest, how you dress, how you pray
  • Shabbat is observed regardless of theological nuance — it is a DOING, not just a believing
  • Kashrut is embodied practice — every meal is a religious act
  • Bar/Bat Mitzvah = taking ON commandments (mitzvot = doing), not a profession of faith
  • Humanistic Jews are atheists who still practice Jewish culture — you can be Jewish without belief
  • Rabbinic tradition: debating HOW to observe is more developed than debating WHAT to believe

Evidence AGAINST (belief matters too)

  • The Shema is a belief declaration: "The Lord is One" — radical monotheism IS a doctrinal position
  • Maimonides' 13 Principles of Faith include belief in God, Torah, Messiah, resurrection
  • Teshuvah requires genuine inner turning — not just external performance
  • The covenant is relational — it requires faith as well as action

Nuanced Conclusion

Judaism is best understood as orthoprax (right practice) rather than orthodox (right belief) in its emphasis — but practice IS belief. Every act of Kashrut, every Shabbat candle, every Tzedakah payment enacts a theology. In Judaism, believing through doing is the norm. The statement is largely accurate, but overstated — practice and belief are inseparable in Jewish life.

📄 Paper 1 — Source Analysis (Judaism)

Read the sources carefully. Answer all questions. Refer to the sources in your answers.

Source A — Primary Text

"Hear, O Israel: The Lord our God, the Lord is one. Love the Lord your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your strength. These commandments that I give you today are to be on your hearts. Impress them on your children. Talk about them when you sit at home and when you walk along the road, when you lie down and when you get up. Tie them as symbols on your hands and bind them on your foreheads. Write them on the doorframes of your houses and on your gates."

Deuteronomy 6:4–9 (The Shema and V'ahavta) — Torah, written c. 7th–5th century BCE
Source B — Scholarly Commentary

"Judaism is fundamentally a religion of deed, not creed. The 613 commandments bind the Jew to a covenant relationship with God that is enacted through every dimension of daily life — what one eats, how one rests, how one treats the stranger. Belief without action is, in the Jewish tradition, meaningless. The Shema is not merely an assertion; it is a summons to total, embodied commitment. To bind the words upon one's hands and head is to make belief physical, observable, and communal."

Dr. Rachel Adler, Jewish Studies scholar, "Engendering Judaism," 1998

1a. According to Source A, identify THREE ways the commandments should be transmitted and observed. [3 marks]

3 marks — 1 per valid point
Model Answer:
  • They should be on your heart (internal/personal observance)
  • Taught to children (transmission to next generation)
  • Tied on the hands and bound on the forehead (worn physically — tefillin)
  • Written on doorframes (mezuzah — physical marker of Jewish home)
  • Talked about at home, walking, lying down, getting up (constant, pervasive observance)

1b. Analyse how Source A reflects the Jewish understanding of the covenant (Brit). [5 marks]

5 marks — analysis required
Model Answer:
  • Obligation: The commandments are presented as requirements, not suggestions — "these commandments I give you today are to be on your hearts." The covenant demands total commitment.
  • Relationship: The Shema begins by naming the relationship — "The Lord OUR God" — emphasising the communal, covenantal dimension. This is not a personal individual contract but a communal one (addressed to "Israel").
  • Embodied covenant: The requirement to tie commandments on hands and head (tefillin) and write them on doorframes (mezuzah) shows that the covenant is enacted physically — it is not merely internal belief.
  • Generational transmission: "Impress them on your children" — the covenant extends across generations. Each generation renews and continues the covenantal relationship established at Sinai.
  • Total commitment: "All your heart, soul, and strength" — the covenant demands every dimension of self, not partial allegiance.

1c. Using Sources A and B and your own knowledge, evaluate the claim that Judaism is "a religion of deed, not creed." [5 marks]

5 marks — evaluation + own knowledge
Model Answer:
  • Supporting evidence (Sources): Source B explicitly argues Judaism is about "deed not creed" — 613 mitzvot govern daily life. Source A shows commandments made physical (tefillin, mezuzah) — belief is enacted through action.
  • Supporting evidence (own knowledge): Kashrut, Shabbat, Brit Milah — all bodily, enacted practices. Bar/Bat Mitzvah = taking on commandments (doing). Humanistic Jews are non-believing but practice Jewish culture — suggesting practice can exist without creed.
  • Counter-evidence: The Shema IS a creedal statement — "The Lord is One" is a doctrinal claim of absolute monotheism. Maimonides' 13 Principles of Faith include belief in God's unity, Moses' prophecy, the resurrection of the dead. Teshuvah requires inner conviction, not just external action.
  • Nuanced evaluation: The claim is largely accurate in emphasis — Judaism is unusually practice-centred compared to Christianity. But practice and belief are inseparable in Jewish thought; every act embodies a theology. Source B's Dr. Adler acknowledges this: making belief "physical" means belief is present, just embodied differently.
  • Conclusion: Judaism prioritises orthopraxis (right practice) over orthodoxy (right belief), but this does not mean belief is absent — it is enacted through practice. The dichotomy is false; deed IS creed in Judaism.

1d. Using Sources A and B and your own knowledge, discuss the significance of sacred texts in shaping Jewish religious identity. [10 marks]

10 marks — extended response
Model Answer — Key Points to Include:
  • Torah as foundation of identity: The TaNakh — especially the Torah — is the constitution of Jewish life. Identity is shaped by the covenant narrative: Abraham, Exodus, Sinai. Being Jewish means being part of this story.
  • The Shema as identity marker: Source A — recited twice daily, at bedtime, and upon death — the Shema is THE core identity declaration. "Hear, O Israel" addresses the community collectively; "The Lord is One" defines the monotheistic commitment that makes Jews distinct.
  • Talmud and living identity: The Talmud (Mishna + Gemara) shows that Jewish identity is dynamic — it is a community of interpreters, always debating how to apply the Torah. The "fence around the Torah" keeps identity intact across centuries and geographies of the Diaspora.
  • Denominations and text: How denominations relate to the Torah defines their identity. Orthodox: divinely revealed, unchanging. Reform: humanly written, evolving. The same text produces different communities — showing text is a living identity-shaping force, not a fixed object.
  • Diaspora and text: After 70 CE, Jews had no Temple and no land — but they carried the Torah. The text BECAME the homeland. This is crucial: Jewish identity survived 2,000 years of exile because of textual transmission.
  • Physical texts as identity: Torah scrolls are handwritten with specific requirements; they are dressed, carried in procession (Simchat Torah). The text is itself a sacred object — kissed, undressed before reading. Physical relationship with the text is part of identity.
  • Source B's point: Sacred text shapes identity by requiring embodied response — tefillin, mezuzah, Kashrut — the text literally gets written on bodies and homes. Identity is carved out of daily life by textual commandment.
  • Conclusion: Sacred texts are the spine of Jewish identity — they provide narrative (who we are), law (what we do), and community (who we are with). The Torah has kept Jewish identity intact across millennia of persecution, diaspora, and change — a remarkable feat that no other institution could have achieved.

Hint: Use TaNakh + Talmud for Judaism. Compare with Guru Granth Sahib (Sikhism) or the Qur'an (Islam). Look at: authority, role in worship, relationship between text and community, interpretation traditions. Always use specific examples from both religions.
3. Examine how the destruction of the Jerusalem Temple in 70 CE transformed Jewish religious practice and belief.
Hint: Before 70 CE — animal sacrifice, Temple, priests (Kohanim). After 70 CE — Synagogue replaces Temple; prayer replaces sacrifice; Rabbi replaces priest. Exile forces God-concept to become universal (God is not limited to a place). This is the birth of Rabbinic Judaism.
4. "Judaism's diversity — from Orthodox to Reform — reveals that there is no single Judaism." Discuss.
Hint: Show real differences: Kashrut, Shabbat, women's roles, "who is a Jew," Torah authority. But identify what all share: TaNakh, covenant narrative, monotheism, Shema, ethical mitzvot. Argue for unity within diversity — or conclude genuine fragmentation. Avoid oversimplification.
5. Discuss the significance of Shabbat and the Jewish festival calendar for maintaining Jewish identity in the Diaspora.
Hint: Diaspora = Jews dispersed without a homeland or Temple. How does time (Shabbat, annual cycle of festivals) create community and identity across geography? Use specific festivals: Pesach (national memory of Exodus), Yom Kippur (moral community), Shabbat (covenant in time). Reference the idea of "a palace in time" (Abraham Joshua Heschel on Shabbat).
