Essential Test Facts

▼
On the IB exam always write "Sanatana Dharma" — never "Hinduism." Sanatana means eternal/everlasting; Dharma means duty/law/way.
Hinduism is henotheistic: belief in one supreme god, while accepting that other deities exist as manifestations of that single deity. It does NOT deny other gods.
Sanatana Dharma is most likely the oldest and most complex of the world religions.
Brahman = ultimate reality; Atman = individual soul/self. In the Upanishads, Atman and Brahman are ultimately identical — this is monism.
🏛

Origins of Sanatana Dharma

▼

Pre-Aryan India (Dravidians / Harappans)

  • Most advanced ancient civilization, developed along the Indus River valley
  • Had a written language (still un-translated)
  • Dravidian yogic practices, gods, and rituals were absorbed into later Hinduism

The Coming of the Aryans

  • "Aryan" is Sanskrit for "noble ones" — began migrating from Iran into the Indus valley in the 2nd millennium BCE after the Harappan collapse
  • Aryan worship was polytheistic (like Greece/Rome): massive sacrifices at open-air altars, use of Soma (a hallucinogenic substance), and horse sacrifice
  • Due to intermingling of Aryans and Dravidians over centuries, it is difficult to separate which practices in the Vedas are Aryan vs. Dravidian in origin

The Caste System (Varna)

  • Aryans created varnas (social classes based originally on skin color)
  • From the Hymn to Purusha (primordial giant torn apart to create the world):
    • Mouth → Brahmins (priests, teachers — highest caste, most spiritually pure)
    • Arms → Kshatriyas (warrior elites, kings, royalty)
    • Legs → Vaishyas (commoners, merchants)
    • Feet → Shudras (servants)
    • Dalits (untouchables) — so impure they are outside the caste system
  • Jati = specific occupational sub-caste within a varna
  • Sva-dharma = personal dharma determined by one's varna and jati
  • Varnashramadharma = duties performed according to one's varna AND ashrama (stage of life)
  • The caste you are born into is based on karma from a past life — it is not about wealth in this life, but about job/spirituality
  • Mobility into higher castes can only happen by reincarnating into them — so perform your duties without attachment

Sacred Texts

▼

Shruti "Revealed" — The Vedas

The oldest sacred books of India; all later texts serve as commentary on them. Origin debated (pre-Aryan? 1500–400 BCE?). Emerged from oral tradition. Author unknown. 4 main books:

  • Rig-Veda (1st, most important): 1,000+ hymns to Aryan gods/mythology — "knowledge or lore"
  • Yajur-Veda (2nd): knowledge of rites — material recited during sacrifices to the gods
  • Sama-Veda (3rd): knowledge of chants — basic hymns chanted by priests during sacrifices
  • Atharva-Veda (4th): home rituals, popular prayers, spells and incantations to ward off evil
In modern Hinduism, Vedic literature is held in highest regard. Almost all Hindus agree on its sacredness — but very few ever actually read them. Mainly for priests, not laypeople. Some say ALL knowledge of the universe is in the Vedas.

The Upanishads (4th section of the Vedas — Shruti)

  • Means "sitting down in front (of a teacher)"
  • The philosophical basis of Hindu thought
  • 200 texts, 1–50 pages each; 14 considered "principal Upanishads"; earliest from 9th century BCE
  • It is WITH the Upanishads that Hinduism shifts to ahimsa — ritual animal sacrifice ends
  • Development of Upanishads coincided with Buddhism and Jainism (mutual influence hard to discern)
  • Core teaching: Atman = Brahman. All existence is illusion (maya) arising from ignorance. The soul (Atman) inhabiting our world is only an expression of Brahman.
  • True knowledge of illusion leads to Moksha — unity with Brahman. Requires many lifetimes.

Smriti "Memorized" — The Bhagavad Gita

  • Best book to read to understand Sanatana Dharma; written 7th–9th century BCE
  • A chapter within the epic Mahabharata; not sacred/revealed but memorized/cultural
  • Four key features:
    1. Spells out religious and social/caste dharma
    2. Openness to multiple paths to Moksha (Jnana, Bhakti, Karma/Sva-dharma) — makes Hinduism the world's most tolerant religion
    3. Vishnu/Krishna as a god who loves and cares for humans
    4. A metaphor for the inner battle of the mind and heart

Smriti The Law of Manu

  • Written by Brahmin priests between 300 BCE and 300 CE
  • Not much read — practiced as a cultural norm, passed down orally through generations
  • Explains the caste system and patriarchal nature of Hindu society
  • Establishes varna-specific roles and duties (dharma); contains roots of many modern Hindu traditions
  • Regulates women's roles: women should not make decisions independently, are for children and home, girls seen as financial liabilities (dowries)
  • Encouraged arranged (child) marriage, suttee/sati (widows burning alive on husband's funeral pyre)

The Human Condition (Samsara, Karma, Moksha)

▼

What is the Human Condition?

Hindus believe humans are caught in samsara — the endless cycle of life, death, and rebirth. The Atman is condemned to favorable or unfavorable reincarnation as a result of karma. Bad karma has a corrupting effect on the universe.

Where Are We Going? (Eschatology)

  • To a favorable or unfavorable reincarnation after death (based on karma)
  • Or to Moksha — liberation from samsara entirely

4 Types of Karma

  • Sanchita Karma: all actions and experiences from ALL past lives the Atman has endured
  • Prarabdha Karma: the portion of Sanchita destined for THIS lifetime — shapes immediate and future experiences in the current life
  • Kriyamana Karma: karma being generated RIGHT NOW in the present moment — shapes future karmic experiences
  • Agami Karma: the karma that will be produced by future actions — the result of decisions made now that will shape future lives

How Do We Reach Moksha? (The 3 Paths)

  • Bhakti Marga: Path of Devotion — devotion to a deity through worship, pilgrimage, prayer
  • Jnana Marga: Path of Knowledge/Wisdom — philosophical study and meditation, understanding Atman=Brahman
  • Karma Marga: Path of Ethical Action — performing one's sva-dharma (caste duties) without attachment

→ Must end ALL karma to reach Moksha. Moksha = unity with Brahman. This is very difficult and requires many lifetimes of much study and no accumulation of new karma.

Ashramas — Life Stages (Law of Manu)

▼
Note: These are generalizations per the Law of Manu. How strictly they are followed varies greatly by gender, caste, wealth, and location.

Men's 4 Ashramas (Upper Caste: Brahmin & Kshatriya)

  • 1st — Brahmacarga (Student): After receiving the sacred thread (Upanayana ceremony). Boy is completely devoted to his studies.
  • 2nd — Householder (Grihastha): In his 20s he becomes financially independent, marries, has children, cares for parents. Not being a householder is considered very taboo.
  • 3rd — Forest Dweller (Vanaprastha): After his children are grown, he begins to withdraw from life and focus on spirituality (if he chooses). May begin focusing on moksha.
  • 4th — Sannyasin (Renunciant/Sadhu): Gives up everything, lives as a solitary beggar in orange robes, frequently found at pilgrimage sites. This is the only stage where one can accrue NO new karma and truly focus on attaining moksha.

Women's 3 Ashramas (All Castes)

  • 1st — Girlhood: Education is household-based (learning to run a home). May receive formal education depending on father/caste/wealth.
  • 2nd — Householder: Devotes herself to husband and children (especially male children). Girls are financial liabilities due to dowries — should be married off as soon as possible.
  • 3rd — Widowhood: Death of husband is blamed on her (bad karma). Wears white, shaves her head, cannot remarry. Vrindavan is a city in India "dedicated" to widows. Historically encouraged to commit sati.
Women have only 3 ashramas (no Sannyasin stage). Per the Law of Manu, women cannot attain Moksha directly.

Purusharthas (4 Goals of Life) & Three Debts

▼

The 4 Purusharthas (Goals/Aims of Human Life)

  • Artha — "What I need": Pursuit of material goods, wealth for family, community, and yourself
  • Kama — "What I want": Enjoyment of pleasures and desires
  • Dharma — "Why am I here?": Your duty according to caste — necessary for order in the world
  • Moksha — "Who am I?": Giving up the other three; full awakening from maya; liberation

The Three Debts

These help preserve harmony in society and with the natural world:

  • To the Gods: Repaid by performing good acts, showing kindness to people in need and all living creatures
  • To the Sages: Repaid by studying sacred texts, gaining and passing on knowledge to the next generation. Performing sacrifices (e.g., volunteering time at a temple) helps repay this debt.
  • To the Ancestors: Repaid by honoring the family reputation, carrying on the family by producing children

Deities — The Trimurti & Beyond

▼
Remember: Hinduism is henotheistic. All devas and devis are "married" — meaning they are manifestations of one ultimate reality, not separate beings in a Western romantic sense.

The Trimurti

  • Brahma (Creator): Creator of the world. Receives the least attention — only one temple in India. No cult of devotees. Red with four bearded faces and four arms. Not mentioned in the Vedas.
  • Vishnu (Preserver): Love, benevolence, forgiveness. Appears on Earth in various forms (avatars — usually Krishna or Lord Rama). The 10th avatar will occur at the end of the age to punish the wicked and reward the virtuous. Sects following Vishnu believe he is the ultimate reality. Worshipped at Varanasi.
    ⚠️ Hare Krishnas (ISKCON) devote themselves to Krishna — common in American cities, NOT mainstream in India. Beware of "cults" and false gurus disguised as Sanatana Dharma.
  • Shiva (Destroyer): Death, destruction, disease, and reproduction. Not in Vedic literature — thought to have evolved from Rudra. Some sects (Shivaites) believe he is the ultimate reality. Worshipped at Varanasi.

The Divine Feminine — Shakti & Devi

  • Shakti: Divine feminine creative power
  • Devi: The female goddess — can be depicted as all-loving and gentle, OR lustful and angry
  • Shaktism: Devotion to the goddess — extremely common across all castes and genders
  • India as a land is considered a goddess — Bharat Mata (Mother India) — so pilgrimages (yatras) are very important; almost all of India is accessible as a holy site
🪔

Devotion, Practices & Holy Days

▼

Common Forms of Bhakti (Devotion)

  • Achamana: ritual purification
  • Puja: worship/prayer (in the home or at a mandir/temple)
  • Murtis: images of god used in worship
  • Darshan: the state of seeing and being seen by the deity — the holiest form of encounter
  • Yatra: pilgrimage (the ultimate form of darshan). Many believe if you die on pilgrimage/yatra at Varanasi you attain moksha automatically
  • Puranas: mythology used in devotion
  • Community work, chanting, celebrating festivals

The Religious Experience (6 steps)

  • 1. Fix mind on the glory of god (like a religious retreat)
  • 2. Spiritual development → reading sacred text
  • 3. Purification of pap (sin)
  • 4. Guidance from holy people/guru
  • 5. Perform rites and rituals
  • 6. Pilgrimage as the ultimate form of darshan

Sansakras (Key Rituals)

  • Upanayana (Thread Ceremony): For 3 upper castes (male only). Makes them "twice born." Brahmins age 7, Kshatriyas age 10, Vaishyas age 11.
  • Weddings: Very large, elaborate, multi-day, expensive events. Arranged marriages still very common. "Non-traditional" weddings vary by location.
  • Death: Immediate cremation to release the Atman. Suicide is very bad karma. Euthanasia/assisted suicide is up for debate.

Hindu Holy Holidays

Practiced quite differently between North and South India. Vary greatly by branch, caste, gender, status of god, and location.
  • Holi (Feb/March): Welcome spring, honor Krishna. Celebrates destruction of demons. Many caste/taboo restrictions set aside. Kama (pleasure) emphasized.
  • Diwali (November): Festival of lights / Hindu New Year. Kali and Lakshmi honored. Devotees make pilgrimages to holy sites.
  • Dasara/Navratri: Celebrates Shakti. Nine days in October to honor Durga and her victory over the Buffalo Demon.

Ashrams & Gurus

Ashrams are places to meditate/study — they also serve as homeless shelters for widows. The role of the guru is to study with students. Meditation, hatha yoga, and other practices are done at ashrams.

Dietary Laws & The Cow

  • There are NO mandatory dietary laws
  • Veganism is highly encouraged, especially for Brahmins — eating animals is considered bad karma
  • The cow is NOT worshipped. It is NOT a goddess. It is respected, honored, and adored — it represents Bharat Mata, the ultimate all-giving, caring mother

"Examine the role of karma in Sanatana Dharma and how it shapes the human condition."
▼
1

Introduction — Define & Frame

  • Define karma: the law of action — every action and thought has a consequence, "paid" in the next life
  • Note that karma is central to Sanatana Dharma's cosmology: it governs samsara (cycle of rebirth), social structure (varna/caste), and the path to moksha
  • Thesis: karma in Sanatana Dharma is not simply cause-and-effect but a comprehensive spiritual, social, and ethical framework
2

Karma & Samsara (Human Condition)

  • Hindus believe the Atman is condemned to samsara as a result of accumulated karma
  • Bad karma = unfavorable rebirth; good karma (punya) = favorable rebirth
  • Bad karma has a corrupting effect on the entire universe — not just the individual
  • Reference the 4 types: Sanchita, Prarabdha, Kriyamana, Agami
3

Karma & the Caste System

  • The varna (caste) one is born into reflects karma from past lives
  • Caste determines sva-dharma (personal duty) — fulfilling caste duties without attachment generates good karma (Karma Marga path)
  • Law of Manu: varna mobility only through reincarnation — perform duties without attachment
4

Karma & the Path to Moksha

  • Moksha requires ending ALL karma — Sanchita karma must be exhausted
  • Karma Marga: performing ethical actions without attachment to results reduces karmic accumulation
  • The Sannyasin stage is the only life stage where one can accrue NO new karma
  • Atman-Brahman unity (taught in Upanishads) is achieved when all karma is released
5

Evaluation & Conclusion

  • Evaluate: karma provides a comprehensive ethical, eschatological, and social framework
  • Critique: the doctrine can justify social inequality (caste) as deserved from past lives
  • Conclude: karma is arguably the most foundational concept in Sanatana Dharma, linking the human condition, social order, and ultimate liberation
Key Terms to Use: karma, Sanchita, Prarabdha, Kriyamana, Agami, samsara, moksha, Atman, Brahman, punya, pap, sva-dharma, varnashramadharma, Karma Marga, Sannyasin
"To what extent does the caste system reflect core beliefs of Sanatana Dharma?"
▼
1

Introduction

  • Define varna (social classes based originally on skin color), jati (occupational sub-caste), sva-dharma
  • Contextualise: caste originates from the Hymn to Purusha (Aryan period) and is codified in the Law of Manu
  • Thesis: the caste system is deeply embedded in core Hindu beliefs (karma, dharma, samsara) but its modern practice is contested
2

Caste & Core Beliefs (YES, it reflects core beliefs)

  • Karma determines birth caste — caste is the spiritual result of past lives, not arbitrary social assignment
  • Dharma: each varna has specific sva-dharma; fulfilling these duties upholds cosmic order (rita)
  • Varnashramadharma: duties performed according to varna AND ashrama — a total framework for a righteous life
  • Bhagavad Gita endorses caste dharma as a valid path to Moksha (Karma Marga)
3

Complications & Critiques (TO WHAT EXTENT)

  • The Upanishads teach Atman = Brahman — spiritually ALL souls are identical; caste is maya (illusion)
  • The Bhakti movement (Bhakti Marga) allows all castes to reach moksha through devotion — undermines caste hierarchy
  • Henotheism: all are manifestations of Brahman — suggests spiritual equality
  • The Dalit (untouchable) system is seen by many scholars as a social corruption, not a core spiritual teaching
4

Conclusion

  • The caste system reflects core Hindu beliefs in karma, dharma, and duty to a significant degree
  • However, the Upanishadic and Bhakti traditions suggest that spiritual equality is equally fundamental
  • The tension between social hierarchy (Law of Manu) and spiritual equality (Upanishads) is unresolved
Key Terms to Use: varna, jati, sva-dharma, varnashramadharma, karma, dharma, Atman, Brahman, maya, Dalit, Law of Manu, Hymn to Purusha, Bhakti Marga, Karma Marga
"Analyze the significance of sacred texts in the belief and practice of Sanatana Dharma."
▼
1

Introduction

  • Distinguish Shruti (revealed/sacred) from Smriti (memorized/human-composed)
  • Name the key texts: Vedas, Upanishads, Bhagavad Gita, Law of Manu
  • Thesis: sacred texts shape Hindu beliefs at every level — cosmology, ethics, social order, and spiritual path
2

The Vedas — Foundation of Shruti

  • Oldest texts; 4 books: Rig, Yajur, Sama, Atharva Vedas
  • Almost universally regarded as sacred — but rarely read; mainly for priests
  • Established Aryan religious practices: sacrifice, hymns, rituals, Soma
  • "Some say all knowledge of the universe is in the Vedas"
3

The Upanishads — Philosophical Revolution

  • The 4th section of the Vedas — shift from ritual to philosophy
  • Introduced Atman = Brahman; maya; and the goal of Moksha
  • Marks end of animal sacrifice — introduction of ahimsa
  • Influenced and was influenced by Buddhism and Jainism (difficult to discern which way)
4

Bhagavad Gita — The People's Guide

  • Most accessible guide to Sanatana Dharma; from the epic Mahabharata (Smriti)
  • Teaches three valid paths to Moksha — makes Hinduism tolerant/inclusive
  • Vishnu/Krishna as a loving, caring deity — personal relationship with god
  • Inner spiritual battle metaphor resonates across generations
5

Law of Manu — Social Prescription

  • Not "read" but lived — passed down orally and practiced as cultural norm
  • Shapes caste duties, gender roles, marriage practices, social structure
  • Contested in modern Hinduism — sati outlawed, women's rights expanded
6

Conclusion

  • Sacred texts shape belief (cosmology, eschatology) AND practice (ritual, caste, gender roles)
  • The tension between Shruti authority and Smriti practicality is ongoing
  • Most Hindus live by the Smriti texts culturally while revering Shruti spiritually
Key Terms to Use: Shruti, Smriti, Vedas, Upanishads, Bhagavad Gita, Law of Manu, ahimsa, Atman, Brahman, maya, moksha, dharma, karma
"Discuss the role of women in Sanatana Dharma, with reference to both traditional teachings and practice."
▼
1

Introduction

  • Women's roles in Sanatana Dharma are shaped by the Law of Manu, the ashramas, and the varna system
  • Thesis: traditional teachings restrict women significantly, but the divine feminine (Shakti/Devi) and some paths to moksha offer a counterbalancing spiritual status
2

Restrictions — Law of Manu & Ashramas

  • Women should never make decisions independently; should not seek independence from husband and children
  • Girls are financial liabilities (dowries) — should be married off as soon as possible
  • Only 3 ashramas (no Sannyasin stage); traditionally cannot attain moksha directly
  • Widowhood: husband's death blamed on wife; expected to commit sati historically; wears white, shaves head, cannot remarry
  • Vrindavan: city "dedicated" to widows
3

Spiritual Elevation — Shakti & Bhakti

  • The divine feminine (Shakti, Devi) is central to Hindu worship — Shaktism is extremely popular
  • India as Bharat Mata (Mother India) = land is itself a goddess
  • Bhakti Marga is open to ALL castes and genders — women CAN pursue moksha through devotion
  • Female deities (Lakshmi, Durga, Kali, Saraswati) are powerful and widely worshipped
4

Modern Practice

  • Arranged marriages still common globally but sati outlawed in India
  • Women's rights have expanded significantly in modern India
  • How strictly ashramas are followed varies greatly by caste, wealth, region, and personal choice
5

Conclusion

  • Traditional texts (Law of Manu) restrict women socially and spiritually
  • Yet the divine feminine is central and the Bhakti path offers women real spiritual agency
  • Tension between textual prescription and lived reality defines the discussion
Key Terms to Use: Law of Manu, ashramas, sati, sva-dharma, Shakti, Devi, Bhakti Marga, moksha, Bharat Mata, pap, punya, Vrindavan, henotheism
"Examine how Sanatana Dharma addresses the question of ultimate liberation (moksha)."
▼
1

Introduction

  • Define moksha: liberation from samsara, ending karma, unity with Brahman
  • Context: moksha is the 4th and ultimate Purushartha (aim of human life)
  • Thesis: Sanatana Dharma offers multiple paths to moksha, making liberation theoretically accessible to all
2

The Problem: Samsara & Maya

  • The Atman is trapped in samsara — cycles of rebirth determined by karma
  • Maya (illusion) keeps us from perceiving Brahman — all we see, hear, touch is illusion
  • Ignorance of the true nature of reality (Atman = Brahman) perpetuates samsara
3

The Three Paths to Moksha

  • Jnana Marga: True knowledge of illusion is realized — one becomes liberated from life and achieves unity with Brahman. Requires many lifetimes, much study, and generating no karma. Very difficult.
  • Bhakti Marga: Devotion to a deity — through puja, yatra, darshan. Open to all. A pilgrimage death at Varanasi is believed to grant instant moksha.
  • Karma Marga: Ethical action without attachment. Fulfilling sva-dharma without desire for fruit. Taught prominently in the Bhagavad Gita.
4

The Sannyasin & Ashramas

  • The 4th ashrama (Sannyasin/Sadhu) is the only life stage allowing no accumulation of new karma
  • Orange robes, solitary life, pilgrimage sites — giving to a Sadhu accrues good karma for the giver
  • Women (per Law of Manu) cannot reach the Sannyasin stage — debates about women's access to moksha
5

Conclusion

  • Moksha is the ultimate goal of Sanatana Dharma — liberation from the illusion of maya into union with Brahman
  • Multiple paths reflect the tolerance and inclusivity of the tradition
  • Achieving moksha is extremely difficult and typically requires many lifetimes
Key Terms to Use: moksha, samsara, maya, Atman, Brahman, Jnana Marga, Bhakti Marga, Karma Marga, karma, Sannyasin, Sadhu, darshan, yatra, Varanasi, Upanishads, Purusharthas

Paper 1 — Source-Based Analysis

  • You will be given a source (text, image, or description) related to a religion you have studied
  • Questions test your ability to identify, explain, and analyze religious concepts from the source
  • Question types: define, explain, analyze, evaluate — each with increasing marks
  • Always use correct religious terminology ("Sanatana Dharma" not "Hinduism")

"A young man leaves his family home at the instruction of his father. He is taken to the family Brahmin priest who performs a ceremony, and the boy is given a sacred thread to wear around his body. He is told he must now devote himself entirely to his studies under the guidance of a guru. He must observe celibacy, speak truthfully, and honour his teacher. He is no longer simply a child — he has become 'twice born.'"

Q1(a) [2 marks]
Identify the ritual being described in the source and name the group for whom it is intended.
The ritual described is the Upanayana (thread ceremony / sacred thread ceremony). It is intended for males of the three upper varnas (castes): Brahmins (age 7), Kshatriyas (age 10), and Vaishyas (age 11). The ritual marks the boy as "twice born" — born once physically, and a second time into his spiritual/religious life. It is a Sansakra (rite of passage).
Q1(b) [4 marks]
Using the source and your own knowledge, explain what the term "twice born" means in Sanatana Dharma, and why this stage of life is significant.
"Twice born" (dvija) refers to males of the three upper varnas who have undergone the Upanayana ceremony. The first birth is biological; the second birth is spiritual/religious — the boy is now initiated into his dharmic obligations and is eligible to study the Vedas and perform religious rites.

This stage is the 1st ashrama (Brahmacarga — the student stage) as described in the Law of Manu. The boy must be completely devoted to his studies, observe celibacy, speak truthfully, and serve his guru. This stage is foundational because it establishes his sva-dharma (personal duty according to caste). It also links to the concept of varnashramadharma — duties performed according to one's varna AND stage of life. Without completing this stage properly, a man cannot fulfill his dharmic responsibilities in later life stages (householder, forest dweller, renunciant), making it critical for the eventual pursuit of moksha.
Q1(c) [6 marks]
Using the source and your own knowledge, analyze how the concept of dharma governs the life of a Hindu male according to Sanatana Dharma.
Dharma in Sanatana Dharma means duty, law, and way — but it operates at multiple levels: cosmic order, social obligation, and personal spiritual responsibility.

1. Varnashramadharma: The Law of Manu establishes that every male of the upper castes has duties defined by both his varna (social caste) and his ashrama (life stage). The source illustrates the 1st ashrama (Brahmacarga) where the boy's dharma is complete devotion to study under a guru.

2. The 4 Ashramas: As a man progresses through life, his dharma changes: from student (celibacy, study) to householder (marriage, children, work) to forest dweller (withdrawal from worldly life) to sannyasin (full renunciation, pursuit of moksha). Each stage has specific duties that must be fulfilled.

3. Karma & Dharma: Fulfilling one's dharma generates good karma (punya); failing in it generates pap (sin/bad karma). This connects dharma directly to eschatology — how well one performs dharmic duties determines the quality of future rebirths or progress toward moksha.

4. The Bhagavad Gita teaches that performing one's caste duty (sva-dharma) without attachment to results is itself a path to moksha (Karma Marga). This makes dharma not merely a social obligation but a spiritual practice.

In conclusion, dharma in Sanatana Dharma is a total framework governing a Hindu male's spiritual, social, and ethical life from birth to death.

"After many years of devoted study under her guru, the young woman came to understand that the world around her — the beauty of the temples, the festivals, the very gods she worshipped — were all expressions of a deeper, unchanging truth. She understood that her own soul was not separate from this ultimate reality. In that moment of understanding, she felt all desire and fear dissolve. This, she understood, was what the ancient teachers had pointed toward."

Q2(a) [2 marks]
Identify the spiritual state being described in the source and name the Sanskrit term for it.
The source describes moksha — liberation or release from samsara (the cycle of death and rebirth). It is also the realization that Atman = Brahman (the individual soul is identical to the ultimate reality). The woman's understanding that the world she sees is "expressions of a deeper, unchanging truth" refers to maya (illusion) dissolving. This spiritual insight is the goal of the Jnana Marga (path of knowledge/wisdom).
Q2(b) [4 marks]
Using the source and your knowledge, explain the concept of maya in Sanatana Dharma and its relationship to the goal of liberation.
Maya is the illusion — the illusory, created forms of the world that our senses (gunas) perceive. Everything we see, hear, and touch is maya; it is like a dream or an illusion that obscures the true nature of reality: Brahman.

In the Upanishads, the foundational philosophical texts of Sanatana Dharma, it is taught that all phenomenal existence is illusion arising from ignorance (avidya). The living soul (Atman) inhabiting our body is actually identical to Brahman (ultimate reality), but maya creates the appearance of separateness.

Relationship to moksha: Liberation (moksha) can only be achieved when one sees through maya and realizes Atman = Brahman. This is what the source describes — the woman's realization that even the gods and festivals she worshipped were "expressions of a deeper, unchanging truth." Once this knowledge is achieved and all karma is exhausted, the Atman is "released" from samsara and merges with Brahman. This is the ultimate goal — the 4th Purushartha — of Sanatana Dharma.

Paper 2 — Extended Essay Questions

  • Choose one question from a set of thematic options
  • Essays should be approximately 800–1,000 words (in the actual exam)
  • Must demonstrate: knowledge, understanding, analysis, evaluation, and use of religious terminology
  • Always write "Sanatana Dharma" — not "Hinduism"
  • Include specific examples (texts, rituals, beliefs) and evaluate multiple perspectives
Question 1 [30 marks]
"In Sanatana Dharma, the caste system is a spiritual framework, not merely a social one." To what extent do you agree with this statement?
Agree (spiritual framework arguments):
• Varna originates from the Hymn to Purusha — a sacred cosmological myth; caste is rooted in divine creation
• One's caste reflects karma from past lives — a deeply spiritual concept (not arbitrary social assignment)
• Varnashramadharma links caste duty to spiritual progress; fulfilling sva-dharma is Karma Marga — a path to moksha
• The Bhagavad Gita teaches that performing caste duty without attachment is spiritually liberating

Disagree (critique/limitations):
• The Law of Manu was written by Brahmin priests — arguably a self-serving social document
• The Upanishads teach Atman = Brahman — suggesting ALL souls are spiritually equal regardless of caste
• Bhakti Marga allows ALL castes (and genders) access to moksha — undermining caste spiritual hierarchy
• The Dalit system has no spiritual justification in the Vedas — it is a social corruption
• Henotheism implies all people are manifestations of Brahman — spiritual equality

Conclusion: Evaluate that caste has BOTH spiritual and social dimensions; the tradition itself is internally divided on whether caste is a spiritual necessity or a social norm shaped by karma.
Question 2 [30 marks]
"The Vedic texts are the most significant sacred writings for the beliefs and practices of Sanatana Dharma." How far do you agree?
Agree — significance of the Vedas:
• Oldest and most authoritative — Shruti (revealed); all other texts are commentary
• Almost universally regarded as sacred by all Hindus; "all knowledge of the universe is in the Vedas"
• Established foundational practices: ritual, sacrifice, cosmology, hymns to deities
• The Upanishads (4th section of Vedas) provide the philosophical basis of Hindu thought: Atman=Brahman, maya, moksha

Disagree — other texts may be more significant in practice:
• Vedas are mainly for priests — rarely read by laypeople; "mainly for priests not lay people"
• Bhagavad Gita (Smriti) is described as the BEST book for understanding Sanatana Dharma — more widely known and loved
• Law of Manu (Smriti) actually governs daily life, caste duties, gender roles, marriage — more practically significant
• Upanishads (technically Vedic but often discussed separately) more philosophically influential than other Vedic hymns

Nuanced conclusion: The Vedas are symbolically and spiritually supreme, but in lived practice the BG and Law of Manu may have greater day-to-day influence. The distinction between Shruti authority and Smriti practical application is key.
Question 3 [30 marks]
"Sanatana Dharma offers no single path to moksha, and this is both its greatest strength and its greatest weakness." Discuss.
Strength of multiple paths:
• Bhakti Marga: accessible to ALL castes and genders through devotion — the most democratic path
• Jnana Marga: for the philosophically inclined — deep study and meditation
• Karma Marga: ethical action without attachment — a practical, everyday spiritual life
• The BG's openness to all three paths makes Sanatana Dharma "the world's most tolerant religion"
• Henotheism allows worship of many deities — people can connect to Brahman through whatever form resonates

Weakness of multiple paths:
• Lack of doctrinal unity creates internal contradictions (e.g., caste hierarchy vs. spiritual equality)
• Different castes and genders have unequal access to certain paths (Jnana mainly for upper castes; women excluded from Sannyasin stage)
• Risk of syncretism and dilution — hard to define what Sanatana Dharma "is" given its diversity
• "Cults" and false gurus can exploit this flexibility

Conclusion: The flexibility of Sanatana Dharma is genuinely its most distinctive feature; evaluate whether it produces spiritual inclusivity or theological incoherence.
Question 4 [30 marks]
"The concept of dharma is the most important concept in Sanatana Dharma." Evaluate this claim with reference to other key concepts.
Dharma IS the most important (agree):
• Dharma = duty, law, way — it underpins the entire social, cosmic, and spiritual order
• Varnashramadharma governs how every person lives from birth to death
• The 3rd Purushartha (Dharma) is essential for maintaining order in the world — without it, chaos
• Even the paths to moksha are framed in terms of dharma: Karma Marga = performing dharmic duties; Jnana = understanding cosmic dharma; Bhakti = devotion as duty

Other concepts may be more important:
• Karma: arguably the mechanism that drives everything — determines birth, life circumstances, and spiritual progress
• Brahman/Atman: the ultimate theological reality — the entire purpose of spiritual life is realizing Atman=Brahman
• Moksha: the ultimate goal — without the concept of liberation, dharma would have no ultimate purpose
• Maya: the fundamental problem that dharma and karma are trying to address

Conclusion: Dharma may be the most operational concept — governing daily life — but Brahman/Atman and moksha may be the most theologically fundamental. Argue that they are mutually dependent concepts rather than a hierarchy.
