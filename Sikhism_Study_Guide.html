Historical Context & Origins

▼
  • The Mughal Empire (Islamic rule in India from 1526) created intense tension with native Hindus — religious and political conflict was the backdrop for Sikhism's emergence.
  • Teachers of both religions believed a synthesis could be found. Kabir (1440–1518), not a guru but the best-known reformer, led the Bhakti movement — a Muslim who worshipped alongside Hindu neighbors, preached one God, no caste, social equality.
  • Guru Nanak (1469–1538): Founder of Sikhism, born a Hindu, educated by Muslims, studied with Kabir. While meditating in the forest, God spoke to him: "There is no Muslim and there is no Hindu." This is the founding revelation — NOT a synthesis, but a new divine disclosure.
  • Nanak and his disciple Mardana traveled widely wearing a mix of Hindu and Muslim dress. His followers became known as Sikhs (Punjabi for "disciple"). He founded the first langar.

The Human Condition

▼

All living beings were created by God (Waheguru / Akal Purakh). Through maya (delusion) and haumai (ego-centeredness), the jot (divine spark/soul) is not realized. Human beings therefore remain in the state of manmukh — follower of the ego — focused on the baser emotions summarized as the Five Vices:

Vice 1
Lust (Kam)
Vice 2
Covetousness / Greed (Lobh)
Vice 3
Attachment (Moh)
Vice 4
Anger (Krodh)
Vice 5
Pride / Ego (Ahankar)

The opposite of manmukh is gurmukh — one who is God-centered, focused on God and living by the teachings of the gurus.

Eschatology — Where Are We Going?
  • A person's jot (divine spark) will pass to another living being (samsara, determined by karma) unless they achieve mukti (liberation).
  • Mukti means the jot has entered the Sach Khand (the Realm of Truth) where God is present — but it is God who decides this through Gurprasad (divine grace).
  • There are 5 khands (stages/realms). Humans can progress on their own to the third khand; beyond that, only Gurprasad (grace) can take them. This can happen during a lifetime or at death.
  • Haumai (ego) is the biggest obstacle to reaching mukti — it keeps us trapped in samsara.
  • Everything is according to God's hukam (divine will/command) — including Sach Khand. Sikhs have a responsibility to practice the Three Pillars and become gurmukh, which helps conquer haumai and maya.
How Do We Get There?
  • Become gurmukh (God-centered) through God's grace (Nadar) and living by the teachings of the gurus
  • Practice Nam Japna — continual meditation on God's name
  • Follow the teachings of the Guru Granth Sahib
  • Practice Kirat Karni — earn a living honestly
  • Practice Vand de Chakna — share a portion of earnings with those in need
  • Practice Sewa — altruistic service to God and humanity, regardless of birth or gender

Three Pillars of Sikhism

▼
Nam Japna — Remembrance/meditation on God's name. Includes both Nam Simran (silent, cross-legged meditation on God's name) and Nam Japna (repeating and focusing the mind on God's name, done aloud). This is the spiritual pillar.
Kirat Karni — Earning an honest living from the sweat of one's brow. Work as a form of worship; honest labor is sacred. This is the ethical/economic pillar.
Vand de Chakna — Sharing one's wealth with others; giving a portion to the community. Expressed through the langar (free community kitchen) and charitable giving. This is the social/communal pillar.
Note on Grace vs. Effort: Sikhism holds a tension between hukam (everything is God's will) and human responsibility. Sikhs are called to practice the Three Pillars and be gurmukh — doing so creates the conditions in which God's grace (Gurprasad/Nadar) can operate. Grace is essential; effort is the preparation.

Sacred Texts

▼
Guru Granth Sahib (GGS) — The Primary Scripture
  • Originally called the Adi Granth; compiled by the 5th Guru Arjan. The 10th Guru Gobind Singh added the words of his father (9th Guru Tegh Bahadur) and declared the scripture the eternal guru — thus it became the Guru Granth Sahib.
  • 36 authors — includes hymns by women, Muslims, Hindus, and Kabir. Sikhism believes God may be found in other faiths.
  • Written in Gurmukhi script (Punjabi language). Translations are available; a translated reading is sometimes given after the Gurmukhi reading.
  • All sections have been set to music — all parts can be chanted/sung (kirtan) so everyone can understand.
  • Prakash — the daily ritual of "waking up" the GGS every morning at the gurdwara.
  • Read daily at gurdwaras by a Granthi (reader/narrator — can be a woman).
  • Gurbani — the divinely revealed words of God and gurus uttered by the six human gurus chosen by God, found in the GGS. The authority of the human gurus transferred to the book.
  • Daily Hukamnama — "divine command/divine will": a segment of the GGS randomly chosen every day by the Granthi in Amritsar and broadcast to the worldwide Panth.
Secondary/Non-Canonical Texts (Important but NOT Sacred)
Janam Sakhis — "Life evidences." Four books about the life of Guru Nanak containing miraculous acts and supernatural conversations. Sometimes contradict each other; may have been edited — "similar to the manipulation of the Gospels" (not all Sikhs agree with this characterization).
Dasam Granth — A collection by the 10th Guru Gobind Singh, separate from the GGS. Contains prayers and baptism rites (Nitang Khalsa).
Rehat Maryada (VERY IMPORTANT) — A code of conduct written 1925–1945 by a committee of knowledgeable Sikhs in Amritsar. Includes rules about gurdwaras and personal/communal obligations. Concept dates from the 10th Guru's time. Khalsa Sikhs obey and implement it.
Dharam Yudh (Theory of War) — Military force may be justified if peaceful means to settle conflict have been exhausted. Attributed to the 10th Guru Gobind Singh. The religious/just-war doctrine of the Khalsa.

Rites of Passage
Nam Karan (Naming Ceremony): For mother and child at the gurdwara; no set time after birth. A page of the GGS is randomly opened and the child's name begins with the first letter of the first word on that page.
Amrit Sanskar (Khalsa Initiation): Voluntary initiation into the Khalsa, introduced by the 10th Guru Gobind Singh in 1699. Done when an adherent is ready and understands the commitment. New names: women receive "Kaur" (princess), men receive "Singh" (lion) as middle names. Must wear the 5 Ks thereafter.
Anand Karaj ("Blissful Union"): Sikh marriage ceremony at a gurdwara. Must be between a Sikh man and Sikh woman — both must be practicing Sikhs (not necessarily Khalsa). The couple walks around the GGS four times while the Lavan (wedding hymns) are sung.
Death: Death is a natural process and God's will (hukam). Cremation preferred; ashes put into a river. "A soul is a drop of water and God is the sea." Widows may remarry.
Daily Life & Worship
  • Isnan — Ritual ablutions (cleansing), usually done in the morning. Viewed as both physical and mental cleansing.
  • Ardas — Daily standing 3-part prayer (sung). Done at gurdwara to open/close the GGS (morning, noon, night), and during life rituals (birth, amrit, wedding, death).
  • No weekly holy day — Gurdwara services can be attended any day.
  • Kirtan — Meditative singing of God's word (gurbani). Central to gurdwara worship.
  • Diwan — Formal gurdwara worship service: bow before GGS, reading from GGS, Sikh history, kirtan, prayers, Ardas (standing prayer).
  • Karah Prashad — Sacred sweet food (wheat, ghee, sugar) distributed during religious services as a blessing from God. NOT the same as the langar. Rude to refuse. Done during a religious service — it is a holy offering.
  • Langar — Free communal meal available to anyone regardless of caste, gender, or religion. This is not a religious ritual but an expression of equality and Vand de Chakna.
  • Gurdwara protocol: ritual cleansing (ablutions), remove shoes, cover your head.
Festivals
Vaisakhi — "New Year" celebration; the anniversary of the Khalsa being founded on Earth (1699). DIFFERENT from Hindu/Buddhist Vaisakhi.
Diwali — Celebrates the 6th Guru Har Gobind's release from Mughal captivity. DIFFERENT from Hindu Diwali (return of Rama).
Gurpurab — Commemorative days celebrating the births and deaths of gurus, especially: 1st Guru Nanak, 10th Guru Gobind Singh, and the martyrdoms of the 5th Guru Arjan and 9th Guru Tegh Bahadur.

Ethics, Authority & Social Issues

▼
Authority
  • Guru Gobind Singh declared: consult the GGS for spiritual matters; consult the Khalsa and Rehat Maryada for temporal matters.
  • No priesthood or jurist — authority rests with the GGS, the Khalsa, and/or the Granthi (reader of the GGS).
  • Saints, martyrs (who died for the faith), holy men and holy women also carry moral weight.
Ethical Practice
  • Avoid the Five Vices: lust, covetousness, attachment, anger, pride.
  • No legal code — consult the GGS, Rehat Maryada, and Khalsa for guides to Sikh behavior.
  • Dietary: No kosher or halal meat. If eating meat, only Jhatka — killed with one single, powerful strike to the head. Many Sikhs encourage vegetarianism.
  • Dharam Yudh — Theory of War: military force may be justified as a last resort, after all peaceful means are exhausted.
Gender & Social Equality
  • The GGS and Rehat Maryada state equality in birth and gender — all gurdwara roles can be done by either sex.
  • Reality: Sikhism tends to be very patriarchal, especially at the Golden Temple and many gurdwaras.
  • 3rd Guru Amar Das spoke against sati and purdah — Sikhism was progressive for its time.
  • Homosexuality is "not logical" to Sikh theology, but homosexual/transgender persons are "welcome and accepted" at gurdwaras and considered equal before God. Anand Karaj is generally not performed for same-sex couples despite the GGS not explicitly prohibiting it.
Cosmology

God (Akal Purakh — "Timeless Being") is the creator. The universe and everything that happens is according to His hukam (will). God is Nirguna (formless, one) yet immanent in creation.

"Examine the role of grace (Gurprasad/Nadar) in Sikh soteriology."

▼
Examine the role of grace in the Sikh understanding of liberation. [30]

Introduction

  • Define mukti (liberation), Gurprasad (grace that breaks samsara), Nadar (God's compassionate vision)
  • Thesis: Sikh soteriology holds a productive tension between human responsibility (Three Pillars) and divine grace (Gurprasad) — neither alone is sufficient

The Human Problem — Why Grace Is Needed

  • Maya (delusion) and haumai (ego) trap the jot in samsara
  • Manmukh state: the five vices keep humans bound to rebirth
  • Five khands: humans can progress to the third khand independently; after that, only Gurprasad can carry them to Sach Khand

Human Responsibility — The Three Pillars

  • Nam Japna, Kirat Karni, Vand de Chakna — not means of earning grace but preparations
  • Becoming gurmukh (God-centered): actively choosing God over ego through practice
  • Sewa: selfless service as an expression of the gurmukh orientation

The Role of Grace — Gurprasad

  • Ultimately it is God's will (hukam) who reaches Sach Khand — not guaranteed by human effort alone
  • Nadar: God's compassionate vision is the "eye of God" turned toward humans
  • The Mul Mantra: "By Guru's Grace" — grace is built into the foundational theology
  • GGS as channel of grace: the living guru mediates divine blessings through gurbani, Hukamnama, kirtan

Conclusion / Evaluation

  • Sikh soteriology avoids both pure works-righteousness and passive fatalism
  • Compare: Islamic tawakkul (trust in Allah after human effort) shows a similar tension; Hindu bhakti similarly invites grace through devotion
  • Evaluate: Does the emphasis on hukam undermine moral responsibility? Argue both sides
💡 The tension between hukam (all is God's will) and the Three Pillars (human effort matters) is the most philosophically interesting feature of Sikh theology. Examiners reward engagement with this tension.

"Sikhism is simply a blend of Hinduism and Islam." Evaluate this claim.

▼
"Sikhism is simply a blend of Hinduism and Islam." To what extent is this an accurate description of Sikhism? [30]

Introduction

  • Context: Sikhism emerged in 15th–16th c. Punjab amid Hindu-Muslim tension; Kabir's Bhakti movement sought synthesis
  • Thesis: While Sikhism shares vocabulary and concepts with both traditions, it represents an independent divine revelation — not a synthesis

Evidence That Seems to Support the Claim

  • Monotheism (like Islam) — one formless God, no idols
  • Karma and samsara (like Sanatana Dharma) — rebirth determined by actions
  • Langar and social equality resemble Islamic ummah principles
  • Kabir's Bhakti movement was a direct precursor — Kabir himself a Muslim who worshipped with Hindus
  • GGS includes hymns by Muslims, Hindus, and Kabir — 36 authors

Evidence Against — Why Sikhism Is Its Own Revelation

  • God's message to Guru Nanak: "There is no Muslim and there is no Hindu" — a rejection of both, not a synthesis
  • Rejection of caste (Hindu) AND of halal/ritual slaughter (Islamic)
  • No priesthood — rejects Brahmin monopoly (Hindu) and Islamic jurist traditions
  • The GGS as living guru — no equivalent in either Hinduism or Islam
  • The Khalsa: armed spiritual warriors — no precise equivalent in either tradition
  • Sikhism believes God may be found in OTHER faiths — a pluralistic openness neither Hinduism nor Islam fully shares

Conclusion / Evaluation

  • The "synthesis" label is rejected by Sikhs as reductive — Guru Nanak received an independent revelation
  • Sikhism borrows vocabulary but reframes everything through the lens of Nirguna monotheism and gurmukh ethics
  • Just as Buddhism emerged from Sanatana Dharma but is not "a form of Hinduism," Sikhism is a distinct tradition
💡 This is a classic "evaluate the claim" question. Use evidence from both sides before clearly arguing Sikhism is its own tradition. Quote Guru Nanak's revelation directly.

"Examine how the Guru Granth Sahib functions as a living guru in Sikhism."

▼
Examine the authority and function of the Guru Granth Sahib in Sikh religious life. [30]

Introduction

  • Define: GGS as the 11th and eternal guru — "religion of the book"
  • Context: 10th Guru Gobind Singh transferred guruship to the GGS, ending the line of human gurus
  • Thesis: The GGS functions as a living, active guru — not merely a reference text — through daily ritual, community governance, and spiritual guidance

Composition and Authority

  • 36 authors; originally the Adi Granth; 10th Guru added 9th Guru's words → GGS
  • Gurbani: divinely revealed words — authority of human gurus transferred to the text
  • Written in Gurmukhi (Punjabi script); translated readings sometimes follow
  • Sikhism acknowledges God may be found in other faiths — hence diversity of authors (Muslims, Hindus, Kabir)

The GGS as Active Guru — Daily Rituals

  • Prakash — "waking up" the GGS every morning: treated as a living person
  • Hukamnama — daily random reading broadcast worldwide: the guru speaks daily
  • Diwan — worship service centers on the GGS: bow, reading, kirtan, Ardas
  • Gurdwara = any place where the GGS resides: the guru's presence makes the space sacred

Community Governance & Rites of Passage

  • Nam Karan: child's name drawn from random GGS page — the guru names the child
  • Anand Karaj: couple walks around the GGS during wedding — the guru witnesses
  • Ardas — all major life events opened/closed with prayer before the GGS
  • Temporal authority: consult Khalsa and Rehat Maryada; spiritual authority: always the GGS

Conclusion / Evaluation

  • The GGS is unique in world religions — a scripture treated with the reverence of a living monarch
  • Compare: Torah scrolls in Judaism (respected but not given living-guru status); Qur'an in Islam (divine word but not a guru figure)
  • Evaluate: Does the GGS fully replace human guidance? Note the Rehat Maryada and Khalsa still needed for practical governance

"Examine how the Khalsa expresses core Sikh beliefs."

▼
Examine how the Khalsa and the Five Ks express and reinforce core Sikh beliefs. [30]

Introduction

  • Define Khalsa (initiated Sikh warriors, founded 1699 by 10th Guru Gobind Singh) and Amrit Sanskar (initiation ceremony)
  • Thesis: The Khalsa and 5 Ks embody Sikh theology — equality, spiritual discipline, and the gurmukh ideal — in visible, communal form

Historical Context

  • Founded 1699 in response to Mughal persecution; 6th Guru began armed warrior-saints
  • 9th Guru martyred by Aurangzeb; 5th Guru martyred — the Khalsa is a response to injustice
  • 10th Guru: Dharam Yudh (just war theory) — force justified as last resort

The Five Ks as Theological Symbols

  • Keshi (uncut hair): accepting God's creation as-is; gurmukh submission to divine will
  • Kangha (wooden comb): discipline and order in spiritual life
  • Kachera (cotton underwear): modesty and moral readiness
  • Kara (steel wristlet): the circle of infinity — God's eternity; commitment
  • Kirpan (sword): willingness to defend justice and the faith

Khalsa as Expression of Equality

  • New names: "Kaur" (princess) for women, "Singh" (lion) for men — abolishes caste surnames
  • All Khalsa equal before God and each other — the langar is open to all
  • Initiation is voluntary and based on readiness — not birth

Conclusion / Evaluation

  • The Khalsa is a living embodiment of the gurmukh ideal — God-centered, disciplined, just, equal
  • Evaluate: Does the warrior tradition sit in tension with Sikh egalitarianism? Argue both
  • Compare: Buddhist Sangha (community as expression of Three Jewels) shows a parallel function in different form

"Compare the Sikh and Sanatana Dharma understandings of karma and liberation."

▼
Compare and contrast the Sikh and Sanatana Dharma understandings of karma and liberation. [30]

Introduction

  • Both traditions use karma and samsara; both seek liberation; both originated in India
  • Thesis: Despite shared vocabulary, Sikh and Hindu karma and liberation differ in key ways — particularly on caste, the role of grace, and the nature of God

Karma — Similarities

  • Both: karma = moral actions shaping future rebirths; samsara = cycle of rebirth
  • Both: moral living (dharma/Three Pillars) generates favorable karma

Karma — Differences

  • Sanatana Dharma: karma operates within the varna (caste) system — dharma is caste-specific duty
  • Sikhism: rejects caste entirely — all jots are equal; karma has no caste dimension
  • Sikhism: haumai (ego) is the primary karmic problem, not ritual impurity or wrong-caste action

Liberation — Similarities

  • Both: liberation = escape from samsara; union with ultimate reality
  • Both: devotion and grace play a role (Hindu bhakti; Sikh Gurprasad)

Liberation — Differences

  • Sanatana Dharma (moksha): Atman reunites with Brahman; three paths (Jnana, Bhakti, Karma Yoga)
  • Sikhism (mukti): jot enters Sach Khand — the realm where God is present; achieved through Gurprasad + Three Pillars
  • Sanatana Dharma allows many paths; Sikhism centers on the GGS and gurmukh living
  • Sikh liberation is more explicitly relational — God as Waheguru who sees (Nadar) and grants (Gurprasad)

Conclusion / Evaluation

  • Sikhism shares the frame (karma/samsara) but fills it with radically egalitarian content
  • The caste-free, grace-centered Sikh vision of liberation is a deliberate critique of the Hindu system
  • Evaluate: Which tradition offers a more complete account of the human condition and its resolution?

Question 13 marks
According to Source A, what is the Sikh understanding of God? Identify at least three attributes mentioned.
Source A presents God as: (1) One — "One Universal Creator God," affirming Sikh monotheism (Nirguna — one, formless); (2) Truthful — "The Name is Truth... True in the Primal Beginning... forever and ever True," making truth (Sat) a divine attribute; (3) Beyond fear and hatred — "No Fear. Question 25 marks
Using Source B and your own knowledge, explain how the langar expresses core Sikh beliefs about equality and community.
Model Answer: Source B describes the langar as a free communal kitchen at the Golden Temple where "Sikh and non-Sikh, rich and poor, high caste and low caste" are fed together, sitting on the floor in rows regardless of status. This powerfully expresses several core Sikh beliefs. First, the langar embodies Vand de Chakna (sharing one's wealth) — one of the Three Pillars of Sikhism established by Guru Nanak. By giving freely of food and labor, the community lives out this foundational teaching. Second, the equal seating (pangat — sitting in rows) directly challenges the Hindu caste system, which the early gurus explicitly rejected. The 3rd Guru Amar Das used the langar to require even emperors to sit with the poor before meeting him. Third, the volunteer service (sewa) in the kitchen exemplifies the gurmukh (God-centered) way of life — serving others as an act of worship. Source B notes the langar "is not a religious ceremony" but "an act of service" — and this is significant: the langar's equality is not ritual but practical, embodying Sikh theology in lived community reality. The Panth (worldwide Sikh community) is strengthened by this shared table, which erases social hierarchy in practice.
Question 35 marks
Using Source A and your own knowledge, explain the significance of the Mul Mantra as a summary of Sikh theology. How does it relate to the Guru Granth Sahib's authority?
Model Answer: The Mul Mantra (Seed Mantra) are the opening words of the Guru Granth Sahib (GGS) and are understood as both a summary of all Sikh theology and the basis of Sikh belief. Source A presents it as a compressed theological statement: God is One (Ik Onkar), Truth, beyond fear and hatred, timeless, and self-existent — accessible only "By Guru's Grace" (Gurprasad). Its theological significance is enormous. First, it establishes Nirguna monotheism: God is one and formless — no idols, no caste hierarchy, no intermediaries except the guru. Second, the phrase "By Guru's Grace" introduces Gurprasad at the very opening of the scripture — grace is not peripheral but foundational to Sikh soteriology. The jot (divine spark) can only enter Sach Khand (realm of Truth) through God's grace. Third, the Mul Mantra's position as the opening of the GGS reinforces the GGS's own authority. The GGS is the 11th and final guru — the living, eternal teacher. When the 10th Guru Gobind Singh passed guruship to the GGS before his death in 1708, he was not merely selecting a book; he was declaring that the divine words already contained within it (Gurbani — divinely revealed words) would continue to guide the Panth. The Mul Mantra is the first teaching the living guru speaks — and it speaks of grace.
Question 410 marks
Using both sources and your own knowledge, evaluate how effectively Sikhism addresses the problem of social inequality.
Model Answer (Key Points):

Introduction: Sikhism emerged in 15th–16th century Punjab amid rigid Hindu caste hierarchy and Mughal power. Both sources reflect Sikhism's commitment to equality: Source A's Mul Mantra presents a God "No Fear, No Hatred" — a universal deity transcending social categories. Source B's langar demonstrates this equality in material form.

Theological Basis of Equality: The Mul Mantra (Source A) establishes that God is the one Creator of all — if all are created by the same God, hierarchy is theologically indefensible. Guru Nanak declared "There is no Muslim and there is no Hindu" — rejecting the religious boundaries that structured inequality. The GGS has 36 authors including women, Muslims, Hindus, and Kabir — embodying the belief that God's truth is not the property of any caste or religion. Ik Onkar (God is One) means all humans share one divine origin.

Practical Expressions: Source B shows the langar as a direct challenge to caste hierarchy — shared meals across all social boundaries. The 3rd Guru Amar Das required emperors to sit in pangat (rows) before meeting him. The Khalsa initiative abolished caste surnames: "Kaur" (princess) and "Singh" (lion) replaced caste-identifying names. Vand de Chakna (sharing wealth) and Sewa (selfless service) institutionalize egalitarian practice.

Limitations and Tensions: The GGS and Rehat Maryada state gender equality; all gurdwara roles can be filled by either sex. Yet Source B's own description reflects that women are often disproportionately in service roles, and the Golden Temple is noted as particularly patriarchal in practice. The Anand Karaj is not performed for same-sex couples despite the GGS not explicitly prohibiting it. Conversion is not actively pushed — Sikhism respects other faiths, but this means social change beyond the Sikh community is limited.

Conclusion: Sikhism offers one of world religion's most robust theological and practical frameworks for social equality. Its effectiveness is real — the langar has fed billions across centuries — but the gap between ideal and practice, particularly on gender, remains a live tension that Sikh communities continue to negotiate.
